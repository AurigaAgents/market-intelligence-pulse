# The Blockade Is Real, but the Market Is Already Trading the Deal

## Executive Summary
Global markets on Tuesday pivoted from Monday's stress repricing to a cautious relief trade. The catalyst: oil pulling back nearly 2% as CENTCOM clarified the Hormuz blockade targets Iranian ports only, not all strait traffic. That distinction matters because it compresses the actual supply disruption from a feared 15-20 mbd to roughly 1.5-2 mbd. Equities rallied across every major region, the VIX dropped below 19 for the first time since the blockade announcement, and the most important divergence of the day emerged in sentiment — surveys say extreme fear, but flows say institutional accumulation. The market is now pricing a diplomatic resolution that hasn't arrived yet. That's either smart anticipation or a setup for a sharp reversal if the April 22 ceasefire deadline passes without progress.

## What Drove Markets Today

The first and dominant factor was geopolitical premium compression. After WTI spiked above $104 on Saturday's blockade announcement, Tuesday's pullback to $97 reflects the market digesting a targeted operation rather than full strait closure. CENTCOM's explicit statement that non-Iranian port traffic would not be impeded was the key data point. The headline scared more than the operational scope warrants, and that gap is now closing.

The second factor was the behavior-sentiment divergence. The CNN Fear & Greed Index remains at extreme fear (~15), AAII bearish readings sit at 43%, and the word "crisis" still dominates headlines. Yet the put/call ratio at 0.51 is well below average, equity ETF flows flipped to +$19.2B from -$8.9B in a single week, and FINRA margin debt collapsed $400B from its January peak — clearing out weak hands. This is the textbook setup for a contrarian rally: retail sells, institutions buy.

The third factor was regional differentiation. Japan's Nikkei surged 2.26% because falling oil directly relieves Japan's energy import bill. Europe rallied with the DAX hitting 23,742 as ECB rate-hike expectations compressed from three to two. China's Shanghai Composite reclaimed 4,000 despite a jarring export miss (2.5% vs 8.6% expected), because the market chose to price the 27.8% import surge as a domestic demand recovery signal instead.

The fourth factor was the dollar-metals divergence. Gold barely moved (+0.33%) despite an active military blockade — that tells you the dollar safe-haven bid at DXY 98.79 is absorbing the fear flows that would normally lift precious metals. Silver's sharp -2.4% drop confirms industrial demand fears are outweighing geopolitical hedging.

The fifth factor was rate expectations hardening. The March CPI print at 3.4% YoY has killed near-term Fed easing expectations. Only ~50 bps of cuts are priced through year-end. The April 28-29 FOMC is almost certainly a hold. This creates a floor under yields and a ceiling on duration-sensitive growth names.

## Cross-Market Dynamics
The chain runs in reverse from Monday. Oil lower eased inflation fears, which let yields stabilize, which gave equities room to rally. But the cross-market story is more nuanced than a simple unwind. The dollar held firm even as risk appetite improved — that's unusual and suggests USD strength is structural (fiscal, safe-haven) rather than purely fear-driven. Meanwhile, European banks continued their structural outperformance (UniCredit, BBVA each +~2%) even as energy names lagged, signaling the market is rotating toward rate beneficiaries and away from the crowded oil trade.

The most interesting cross-market signal is the VIX term structure: spot at 18.59 versus May futures at 20.32 in normal contango. The market is not pricing an imminent crash — just sustained uncertainty. That's a very different regime from late March when backwardation signaled acute panic.

## Forward Outlook
The next five trading days are dominated by two catalysts. First, the April 22 ceasefire deadline — this is binary. Extension or progress sends the S&P toward 7,000 and oil toward $90. Breakdown retests S&P 6,500 and oil spikes past $105. Second, earnings season intensifies (Goldman, J&J, UnitedHealth) and will reveal whether energy-driven inflation is compressing corporate margins.

Key levels: S&P 500 resistance at the pre-war high near 7,000. WTI support at $95 (pre-weekend level). VIX at 18 — a sustained break below confirms the regime shift to neutral. Bund yields at 3.13% (March high) — a breach signals fiscal-driven European repricing.
