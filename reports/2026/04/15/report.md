## What Drove Markets Today

**1. US-China Tariff Rollback — The Real Fuel Behind Tuesday's Surge**
The biggest story this week isn't Iran — it's the unexpected US-China trade breakthrough that slashed US tariffs from 145% to 30% and Chinese tariffs from 125% to 10% for a 90-day window. This powered the Dow +1,100 points and Nasdaq +3.2% on April 14. Wednesday's flat open reflects the market having priced the good news in; the question is whether the truce extends. KOSPI's +2.07% and Chinese equities' gains are directly downstream of this deal.

**2. Iran Ceasefire Expiry Approaches — April 21 Is the Binary**
The April 7 ceasefire that sparked a multi-day relief rally faces its first real test in six days. Trump signaled a second round of talks "in the next two weeks," but the Strait of Hormuz remains closed and oil at $93/barrel still carries a war premium. Saxo Bank flagged this as the dominant near-term binary event. The VIX at 18 with futures in contango through September ($21.60–$22.26) tells you the market agrees: this isn't over.

**3. Stagflation Data Hardens — CPI 3.3%, PPI 4.0%, Fed Pinned**
March CPI surged to 3.3% YoY (from 2.4% in February), the largest monthly jump since June 2022. PPI came in softer than feared at +0.5% MoM but the annual rate hit 4.0% — a three-year high. The Fed held at 3.5%–3.75% in March and has nowhere to go: cutting feeds inflation, hiking kills a fragile recovery. This is the definition of a policy trap.

**4. European Luxury Carnage — Hermès -14%, Kering -10%**
Hermès posted its worst single-day loss on record after Q1 revenue showed crushing currency headwinds from a EUR/USD at 1.18 and trade route disruptions from the Hormuz closure. Kering followed at -10%. LVMH reported 1% organic growth but -6% reported revenue on -7% FX impact. The CAC 40 dropped 0.3% while the DAX and STOXX 600 held flat — a clean luxury-specific blowup, not a broad European selloff.

**5. Bank Earnings Wave — JPMorgan's $11.6B Sets the Bar**
JPMorgan posted record Q1 trading revenue of $11.6 billion, and Goldman Sachs delivered earlier this week. Bank of America and Morgan Stanley report today. The trading desks are feasting on volatility — the Iran war, tariff whiplash, and commodity swings are minting money for market-makers. Whether earnings breadth extends beyond banks is the Q2 question.

The DXY at 98 — a multi-month low — is the silent story: dollar weakness benefits US multinationals and EM but is amplifying pain for European exporters (Hermès, LVMH), creating a geographic divergence in who benefits from the risk-on mood.

Commodities tell a more cautious story. Gold at $4,820 is 14% off its January $5,608 peak but still 3x its 2020 level — the safe-haven bid has moderated, not evaporated. Silver's +3.3% outperformance and copper's +30% YoY surge signal industrial demand and tariff front-running, not pure fear. Oil's +1.9% bounce after yesterday's -7.9% crash is a dead-cat reflex until we know if the ceasefire holds.

## Deep Dive

## The Hook

Hermès fell 14% on Wednesday — its worst single-day loss ever — vaporizing roughly $30 billion in market cap before lunch in Paris. Kering dropped 10%. LVMH's Q1 showed 1% organic growth masking a -6% reported decline. The European luxury sector just printed its ugliest session since the 2020 pandemic selloff. This isn't a fashion story. It's a map of how the Iran war, a strong euro, and broken trade routes are converging to hit the world's most profitable companies where it hurts.

## Background

European luxury houses — Hermès, LVMH, Kering — have been the continent's crown jewels for a decade. Hermès alone carried a valuation premium rivaling US tech megacaps, justified by 40%+ operating margins and pricing power that seemed immune to economic cycles. The bull case was simple: wealthy consumers don't stop buying Birkin bags in recessions.

That thesis is being stress-tested by three forces hitting simultaneously. First, the Iran war and Strait of Hormuz blockade have disrupted global shipping routes, raising logistics costs and delaying inventory movement between Europe and Asia — luxury's largest growth market. Second, EUR/USD has climbed to 1.18, the strongest level in years, translating every dollar and yuan of revenue into fewer euros on the income statement. Third, the US-China tariff war (even with the recent 90-day truce) has cooled Chinese consumer spending on Western brands as a matter of both economics and sentiment.

## What the Data Shows

Hermès' Q1 2026 results revealed currency headwinds hitting revenue by an estimated 7-10%, turning what should have been a mid-single-digit organic growth quarter into a miss that shocked analysts. The magnitude of the -14% drop — a record — tells you the market had priced zero probability of this outcome.

LVMH's numbers confirmed the pattern: €19.1 billion in Q1 revenue, up 1% organically but down 6% on a reported basis, with management explicitly calling out a -7% currency impact. Kering's -10% suggests the market is pricing contagion risk — if Hermès can't outrun the macro, nobody can.

The CAC 40 fell 0.3% while the DAX held flat and STOXX 600 barely moved. This is a surgical luxury blowup, not a broad European selloff. But the luxury sector comprises roughly 15% of France's equity market cap — this has index-level consequences if it persists.

## Implications

**Who loses:** European luxury houses with heavy Asian revenue exposure. Hermès, Kering, and LVMH derive 30-50% of revenue from Asia-Pacific. A strong EUR and disrupted supply chains are a double hit. Second-order: French equity funds overweight luxury, European tourism stocks that benefit from wealthy Asian travelers.

**Who wins:** Dollar-denominated luxury and aspirational brands. US-based names like Tapestry and Capri benefit from the same currency dynamics that hurt European peers. Japanese luxury retailers benefit from a weaker yen (158.80) making Japan a discount shopping destination for tourists.

**The bigger signal:** This is the first earnings cycle where the Iran war's economic impact shows up in blue-chip numbers. If Hermès — the most insulated luxury name in the world — is feeling it, the damage to more exposed sectors (industrials, shipping, airlines) will be worse when those companies report.

## Our Take

We expect the luxury selloff to deepen before it stabilizes. The three headwinds — EUR strength, Hormuz disruption, and China cooling — are not cyclical blips; they're structural until the geopolitical backdrop changes. A ceasefire renewal on April 21 would help on the trade-route front but does nothing for the currency or China problems.

The contrarian play: Hermès at -14% in a single day is historically a buying opportunity for the stock specifically — the brand's pricing power and margin structure make it the first to recover when conditions normalize. But "conditions normalizing" requires at least two of the three headwinds to reverse. We're at medium conviction that this is too early to buy the dip, and high conviction that the luxury sector's decade-long premium is being re-rated in real time.

**Conviction level:** Medium-bearish on European luxury sector for Q2 2026. The setup only turns bullish on a confirmed Iran peace deal + EUR/USD reversal below 1.12.
