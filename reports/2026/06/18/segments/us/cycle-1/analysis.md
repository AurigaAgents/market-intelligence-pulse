# US Segment Analysis - Cycle 1 (2026-06-18)

## Overview
US equities rallied broadly in the June 18, 2026 regular session, reclaiming most of the prior day's losses after the United States and Iran signed an initial 14-point memorandum of understanding to end the war and reopen the Strait of Hormuz. The S&P 500 rose 1.1%, the Nasdaq jumped 1.9%, and the Russell 2000 led with a 2.1% gain; the Dow lagged at +0.1%. Crude oil wavered as the deal's supply-chain implications were digested, and Treasury yields eased, partially reversing the spike from the FOMC session. June 18 was the final trading day of the week, with US markets closed Friday for Juneteenth.

## Drivers
The session's primary catalyst was geopolitical: the US-Iran initial deal signed Wednesday. The 14-point memorandum of understanding calls for an immediate end to military operations, the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz to oil tanker traffic, and a 60-day extension of the current ceasefire while negotiations for a permanent resolution continue. For markets, this was a double positive: it removed a major supply-shock risk from the energy sector (crude oil wavered lower, CL-F -0.67%) and it eased the inflationary pressure channel that the Fed had explicitly cited in its June 17 FOMC statement as a driver of the hawkish dot-plot shift.

The risk rally was broad but differentiated. Small caps led (Russell 2000 +2.1%), which is consistent with a risk-on rotation driven by lower energy costs and reduced geopolitical uncertainty — small caps are more domestically oriented and more sensitive to energy input costs. Tech followed (Nasdaq +1.9%), benefiting from the easing in long-end rates. The Dow's near-flat finish (+0.1%) reflects its heavier weighting in energy and industrial names that faced mixed signals from the oil-price wavering, and it had less of the prior day's loss to recover (only -1.0% on June 17 versus the Nasdaq's -1.3%).

The rates backdrop was nuanced. The 10-year Treasury yield fell to 4.44%, partially reversing the prior session's ~5bps increase that followed the FOMC's hawkish projections. The policy-sensitive 2-year edged up to 4.20%, consistent with markets remaining fully priced for a rate hike by October even as the long end eased. This curve behavior — long end falling, front end sticky — is the signature of a geopolitical risk premium being unwound from the long end while the Fed's hawkish policy path continues to anchor the short end.

## Read-Through
For the US segment, the clean signal is "geopolitical relief rally on top of a hawkish Fed baseline." The US-Iran deal removes the energy supply-shock tail risk that the June 17 SEP had explicitly flagged as driving the higher inflation projections. If the deal holds through the 60-day window and the Strait of Hormuz genuinely reopens, the inflation projections that justified the hawkish dot plot may need to be revised back down — which would be equity-positive. But the market is not yet pricing that as the base case: the 2-year yield at 4.20% shows the rate-hike expectation remains fully embedded.

The week-to-date and year-to-date ledgers frame the move well. Despite Wednesday's sharp sell-off, all four major indexes finished the week positive, with the Nasdaq up 2.4% WTD and the Russell 2000 up 1.2% WTD. Year-to-date, small caps remain the clear leader at +20.1%, followed by Nasdaq at +14.1%, S&P 500 at +9.6%, and the Dow at +7.3%. The risk to watch is implementation risk on the Iran deal: the MoU leaves critical issues unresolved (Iran's nuclear program, frozen assets, sanctions relief sequencing, and the Israel-Hezbollah dimension in Lebanon), and the 60-day window is narrow. If the deal frays, the energy-inflation feedback loop that drove the June 17 sell-off reasserts itself quickly.
