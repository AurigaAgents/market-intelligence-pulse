# Sentiment Segment Analysis - Cycle 1 (2026-06-18)

## Overview
US risk sentiment snapped back decisively on June 18, 2026, as the S&P 500 rose 1.08% to 7,500.58, the Nasdaq Composite surged 1.91% to 26,517.93, and the Russell 2000 jumped 2.12% to 2,979.77, erasing most of the prior session's hawkish-Fed sell-off. The VIX collapsed 11.06% to 16.40, unwinding nearly all of Wednesday's 12.37% spike. Three forces drove the rebound: the formal signing of the U.S.-Iran peace agreement and the lifting of the Hormuz blockade, which crushed oil prices and inflation fears; a chip-sector surge on an Apple-Intel manufacturing partnership that sent the Philadelphia semiconductor index up 6.4%; and a modest easing in Treasury yields that supported small caps. Yet the Fed's hawkish signal remained fully priced — CME FedWatch showed ~50% odds of a September hike — and the CNN Fear & Greed Index stayed at 38 ("Fear"), confirming the rally improved the mood but did not erase the damage. All equity and volatility figures are 2026-06-18 US regular-session closes (16:00 ET / 20:00 UTC).

## Drivers
The risk-on reversal had three reinforcing engines. **First, geopolitical de-escalation.** The U.S. and Iran formally signed their peace agreement — a memorandum of understanding extending the April ceasefire by 60 days — at the Palace of Versailles, and U.S. CENTCOM announced the lifting of the naval blockade on the Strait of Hormuz. The first Saudi-flagged supertankers began transiting the strait, and oil prices fell to their lowest level since early March, directly attacking the inflation fears that had driven the prior session's sell-off. (C-2026-06-18-SENT-C1-011)

**Second, a chip-sector catalyst.** President Trump announced that Apple had agreed to work with Intel to design and manufacture chips in the U.S., sending Intel shares up 10.6% to a record close and propelling the Philadelphia semiconductor index 6.4% higher. The rally was broad across the chip complex — SanDisk rose 11.6%, Super Micro gained 10.1% — and the Nasdaq's 1.91% advance was disproportionately attributable to semiconductor and mega-cap tech weights. (C-2026-06-18-SENT-C1-007)

**Third, a yield-driven small-cap bid.** Treasury yields eased across the curve (2-year to 4.19%, 10-year to 4.46%), pulling back from the post-FOMC spike when the 2-year had reached 4.21%. The modest drop in front-end yields was enough to power the Russell 2000 to a 2.12% gain — the day's best major-index performance — as rate-sensitive smaller companies caught a reprieve from the funding-cost pressure implied by the Fed's hawkish dot-plot. (C-2026-06-18-SENT-C1-004, C-2026-06-18-SENT-C1-009)

The critical tell is that the **Fed hawkishness did not unwind.** CME FedWatch held firm at roughly 50% odds of a 25-basis-point September hike and 20% odds of a 50-basis-point move — barely changed from the prior session's post-FOMC reprice. Yet equities rallied anyway. Tony Welch, CIO at SignatureFD, captured the framing: "Markets got spooked by Warsh yesterday essentially promising to contain inflation," but "all together, the package of data is still supportive whether or not the Fed has become a little bit more hawkish." Cantor's Eric Johnston added: "The conclusion today is that the Fed has more credibility around inflation." In other words, the market re-processed the hawkish signal as credibility rather than threat, once the inflationary driver (oil) was defused by the Iran deal. (C-2026-06-18-SENT-C1-010, C-2026-06-18-SENT-C1-011)

## Read-Through
Cycle-1 sentiment reads as a V-shaped recovery from a policy shock, not a regime shift. The tell is the divergence between the VIX (down 11% to 16.40) and the FedWatch hike odds (unchanged at ~50%): the market de-risked the geopolitical tail but kept the policy risk priced. Breadth confirms this — only 5 of 11 S&P sectors rose, meaning the rally was concentrated in tech/chips and cyclicals rather than a broad-based risk-on. The CNN Fear & Greed Index at 38 ("Fear"), up from 32 but far below the 61 ("Greed") of a month ago, reinforces that one session's rebound does not repair sentiment.

The swing factor from here is whether the oil-driven disinflation narrative sustains through the next inflation print. If the Iran deal holds and Brent stays depressed, the Fed's hawkish case weakens and equities have room to grind higher. If the deal frays — Trump himself threatened to resume attacks if Iran fails to honor its commitments — the geopolitical risk premium snaps back and the hawkish Fed overlay re-asserts as a double headwind. With Warsh ending forward guidance and signaling data-dependence, headline sensitivity to each inflation and jobs release should remain elevated. For the broader pulse, this segment flags a market that has re-priced policy risk but is trading on geopolitical headlines — a setup that rewards discipline over conviction.
