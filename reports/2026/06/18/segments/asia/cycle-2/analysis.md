## Overview
Asia split into two clear tracks on June 18, 2026. Japan and South Korea extended record-setting rallies, while mainland China and Hong Kong closed lower. Nikkei Indexes' official daily summary showed the Nikkei 225 at 71,053.49, up 1,151.24 points or 1.65%, with the index crossing 71,000 on a closing basis. The KOSPI closed at 9,063.84, up 199.60 points or 2.25%, its highest close on record and its first finish above 9,000.

The China-linked side of the tape was weaker. Investing.com's Shanghai Composite historical row showed a 4,090.48 close, down 0.43%, with the day range at 4,080.29 to 4,117.45. Trading Economics showed the Hang Seng-linked HK50 at 23,924.81, down 387.35 points or 1.59%, and described the move as a third consecutive loss as Fed-related rate pressure and local caution hit Hong Kong financial, technology, and retail shares.

## Drivers
The positive side was concentrated in Japan and Korea, but the mechanisms were not identical. The Nikkei move was a broad official-index advance, with Nikkei Indexes reporting 136 advancers, 84 decliners, and five unchanged among Nikkei 225 constituents. The Edge Malaysia report tied the Japan rally to improved risk appetite after the US-Iran ceasefire extension and said chip-related shares led, while also noting yen weakness and a hawkish Fed backdrop.

Korea was more concentrated. Reuters, carried by The Star, said Samsung Electronics rose 4.62% and SK Hynix gained 6.51%, both to record closes, and that the two chipmakers accounted for more than half of KOSPI market value. The same source said foreigners bought KRW 1.3 trillion of shares while 791 of 917 traded issues fell. That supports calling the KOSPI move a mega-cap semiconductor-led record, not a broad domestic-equity rally.

Hong Kong moved the other way. Trading Economics attributed the Hang Seng-linked decline to risk aversion after the Federal Reserve's hawkish tone, a stronger dollar and Treasury-yield pressure, and local caution after the Hong Kong Monetary Authority left its base rate at 4.0%. It named financial, technology, and retail shares as pressure points, including Tencent, Meituan, Xiaomi, Lenovo, and Pop Mart International. Shanghai's loss was smaller and is treated here as a daily index-level move rather than a separately sourced policy narrative.
