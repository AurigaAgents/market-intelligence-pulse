## Analysis

June 18's macro signal was not a simple easing story. The energy risk premium came out of crude as the Hormuz route reopened, but policy and FX still carried the prior day's hawkish Fed shock. The official FOMC statement held the federal funds target range at 3.50%-3.75% by a 12-0 vote and said inflation remained elevated partly because of supply shocks including energy. The official projection materials then put the median end-2026 fed funds projection at 3.8%, with 9 of 18 June dots above the current 3.625% midpoint and median 2026 PCE/core PCE inflation at 3.6% and 3.3%. [C-2026-06-18-MACRO-C2-FED-HOLD, C-2026-06-18-MACRO-C2-FED-SEP]

The Treasury curve partially absorbed that shock but did not reverse it. On the official par-yield curve, the 2-year yield was 4.19% on June 18, down 1 bp from June 17; the 10-year was 4.46%, down 3 bp; and the 30-year was 4.90%, down 3 bp. The 2s10s spread therefore flattened to +27 bp from +29 bp. Inference: this was a modest bull-flattening after the Fed repricing, not a clean return to pre-FOMC easing expectations. [C-2026-06-18-MACRO-C2-TREASURY-CURVE]

The dollar confirmed that the Fed signal still mattered. Trading Economics reported DXY at 100.72 on June 18, the highest since May 2025, with the index up 1.39% over four weeks and 1.83% over 12 months. That dollar strength sits awkwardly beside lower crude: the inflation impulse eased at the commodity source, but relative-rate pressure stayed in the currency channel. [C-2026-06-18-MACRO-C2-DXY]

Europe's central-bank decisions added the same nuance. The Bank of England held Bank Rate at 3.75% by a 7-2 vote, but Megan Greene and Huw Pill preferred a 25 bp hike to 4.00%. The minutes put May CPI at 2.8%, projected inflation a little under 3% in Q3 and a little over 3.25% in Q4, and noted Brent around $79 and UK wholesale gas around 100 pence per therm immediately before the meeting. The Swiss National Bank also held, leaving the policy rate at 0.00%, forecasting annual inflation of 0.6% in 2026, 0.6% in 2027, and 0.7% in 2028, and flagging increased willingness to intervene in FX if necessary. [C-2026-06-18-MACRO-C2-BOE-HOLD, C-2026-06-18-MACRO-C2-SNB-HOLD]

The U.S. labor release did not give bond markets an obvious growth scare. Initial claims fell by 4,000 to 226,000 for the week ending June 13, while the four-week average rose to 223,250 and insured unemployment rose by 24,000 to 1.810 million for the week ending June 6. Inference: the claims mix was soft enough to show slower labor absorption, but not weak enough to offset the Fed's inflation-focused posture. [C-2026-06-18-MACRO-C2-JOBLESS-CLAIMS]

Oil was the main relief valve. NYMEX WTI for July delivery settled at $76.60/bbl, down $0.19 or 0.3% after touching $73.58 intraday, while August ICE Brent settled at $79.85/bbl, down $0.30 or 0.4% after a $76.54 intraday low. DTN reported that the U.S.-Iran memorandum appeared to assure immediate toll-free transit through Hormuz, which normally carries about 20 million bpd of petroleum liquids, and that the U.S. military had lifted its blockade on Iranian ports on the strait. [C-2026-06-18-MACRO-C2-OIL-SETTLEMENT, C-2026-06-18-MACRO-C2-HORMUZ-SUPPLY]

Net read: cycle 2 replaces the blocked macro cycle with a clean source ledger. The macro message is a rotation in the pressure point, not a disappearance of pressure. Energy risk eased through crude and Hormuz, but official central-bank sources, the still-flat Treasury curve, and DXY above 100 all point to a market still pricing policy restraint rather than a broad risk-relief easing cycle.
