## Analysis

June 18 was the morning after, and the bond market spent it partially unwinding the hawkish repricing of the Fed day. On the official Treasury par curve the easing was modest but unmistakably long-end-led: the 2-year par yield edged down just 1 basis point to 4.19%, holding the level it had spiked to after the FOMC, while the 5-year fell 4 basis points to 4.23%, the 10-year fell 3 basis points to 4.46%, and the 30-year slipped 3 basis points to 4.90%. Because the belly and long end fell while the policy-anchored front end stood still, the 2s10s par spread bull-flattened 2 basis points to +27 basis points. The shape is the tell: the market still respects the Fed's hike bias in the front end, but it is willing to take back some of the inflation premium it had built into longer maturities the day before. [C-2026-06-18-MACRO-C1-001, C-2026-06-18-MACRO-C1-002, C-2026-06-18-MACRO-C1-003, C-2026-06-18-MACRO-C1-004]

The reason the long end could ease was sitting in the energy market. WTI crude slipped to around $75 a barrel, dipping below $75 to its lowest level since early March, and Brent traded near $79, down about 1.6% on the day, as the U.S.-Iran interim agreement and the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz to tanker traffic drained the war risk premium that had built up through the spring. Maritime authorities guided vessels back through the strait, and crude has now fallen roughly 38% from its April high. This is the single most important macro fact of the session, because it works directly against the Fed's own logic: the June 17 Summary of Economic Projections had explicitly cited Middle East energy risk as a driver of its higher inflation path, and the deal that pulls oil to a three-month low is precisely the development that could force those projections back down. The bond market's long-end rally is the first installment of that argument. [C-2026-06-18-MACRO-C1-008, C-2026-06-18-MACRO-C1-010]

Around the same window, the global central-bank bloc made its own position clear: wait. The Bank of England held Bank Rate at 3.75% in a 7-2 vote, with two of its nine members — reported to be Huw Pill and Megan Greene — dissenting not for a cut but for an immediate 25 basis point hike, as UK CPI held at 2.8% in May and the unemployment rate ticked down to 4.9%. Hours earlier the Swiss National Bank held its policy rate at 0.00%, nudged its 2026 and 2027 inflation forecasts up to 0.6% from 0.5%, and stressed that its readiness to intervene in currency markets has increased, again citing Middle East fragility. Read together with the Fed's hawkish hold the day before, three major central banks in two days all chose to stand pat — and all three named the same swing variable, energy and the Middle East, as the reason they cannot yet declare the inflation fight over. The hawkish dissents in London are the mirror image of the dovish dissents that dominated the prior cycle: the global risk has rotated from "are we too tight" to "are we tight enough." [C-2026-06-18-MACRO-C1-005, C-2026-06-18-MACRO-C1-006]

The dollar refused to follow U.S. yields down. The U.S. Dollar Index rose to 100.72, its highest since May 2025, extending the prior day's advance from 100.47 even as Treasury yields eased. That divergence — a firmer dollar on softer domestic yields — is a rate-differential and safe-haven story rather than a pure yield story: with the Fed the most hawkish of the major central banks and the euro and yen pinned by their own on-hold authorities, the greenback keeps the relative advantage regardless of a few basis points off the U.S. long end. A stronger dollar is itself disinflationary at the margin, reinforcing the same easing impulse the oil move set in motion. [C-2026-06-18-MACRO-C1-007]

The one piece of hard U.S. data cut against the inflation-hawk narrative. Initial jobless claims fell 4,000 to 226,000 for the week ended June 13, roughly in line with the 225,000 consensus, but the details pointed to slow cooling rather than strength: continuing claims rose 24,000 to 1.81 million, and the median duration of unemployment lengthened to 11.6 weeks in May, the longest since November 2021. A labor market that is stable but gradually loosening is not the picture that justifies a hike — and it is exactly the tension the Fed has chosen to resolve in favor of its inflation mandate. [C-2026-06-18-MACRO-C1-009]

Net read: June 18 was a controlled, partial reversal of June 17, and the agent of the reversal was oil. The US-Iran deal and the Hormuz reopening pulled crude to a three-month low, let the long end of the Treasury curve take back some inflation premium, and quietly undercut the very projections the Fed had used to turn hawkish — while the dollar's continued grind higher and a 7-2 hawkish hold at the Bank of England showed that policymakers are not yet ready to act on that relief. The macro story heading into the Juneteenth long weekend is a standoff: a global central-bank bloc anchored to the upside inflation risk from energy, against a market that has just watched the largest single piece of that risk start to deflate. Whichever way crude breaks from here will decide who is right.
