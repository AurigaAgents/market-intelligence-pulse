## Analysis

The June 15 macro tape was a geopolitical de-escalation trade. A tentative U.S.-Iran peace agreement, including a plan to reopen the Strait of Hormuz and restore Persian Gulf oil flows, pulled the risk premium out of energy and ignited a global risk-on rally. The S&P 500 closed at 7,554.29, up about 1.65%, with the move led by a 3.1% jump in the Nasdaq Composite and a near-5% surge in Japan's Nikkei 225. Used here only as a cross-asset risk barometer, that equity strength frames the day; the segment's own signal is in rates, commodities, the dollar, and Fed positioning.

The most informative macro detail is what the bond market did **not** do. Even with equities surging, the Treasury par-yield curve barely moved and drifted slightly lower: the 2-year par yield eased to 4.07% from 4.09%, the 10-year to 4.47% from 4.48%, and the 30-year held at 4.97%. The 2s10s par spread widened by a single basis point to +40, a mild bull steepening as the policy-sensitive front end fell a touch more than the belly. In secondary-market trading the move was a little more pronounced: CNBC reported the on-the-run 10-year note yield sliding to roughly 4.43%, a one-month low, as the oil drop prompted a rethink of near-term Fed rate-hike risk. The official par yield (4.47%) and the secondary-market yield (about 4.43%) are different series by construction, but both point the same way - lower - which is the tell. A genuine risk-on day would normally lift yields; instead the disinflationary pulse from collapsing oil dominated the rates market.

That pulse was large. WTI crude fell about 5.48% to roughly $80.23 a barrel and Brent dropped about 4.95% to roughly $83.01, near a two-month low, as the Hormuz reopening removed a supply-disruption premium that had been propping up energy. Lower crude feeds directly into softer forward inflation expectations, which is precisely why long-dated yields could sit still while stocks rallied.

The dollar and gold completed a consistent picture. The U.S. Dollar Index slipped about 0.19% to roughly 99.56, its lowest in more than a week, as easing Middle East tensions curbed safe-haven demand for the currency. Gold, rather than falling on the risk-on session, rose about 2.77% to roughly $4,338.90 per troy ounce - supported by the softer dollar and lower yields and by positioning ahead of a heavy central-bank week. Gold and equities rising together, with the dollar lower, is the signature of a lower real-rate impulse rather than a simple risk switch.

All of this sets up the June 16-17 FOMC. The federal funds target range stood at 3.50%-3.75% into the meeting, with the decision due Wednesday, June 17 at 2:00 p.m. Eastern. Market pricing put roughly an 89% probability on no change versus about 9% on a 25 basis point cut, so the live question is the bias rather than the level: whether a still-elevated inflation backdrop pushes the Committee's guidance in a more hawkish-hold direction even as oil's slide and the geopolitical thaw argue the other way. The cross-currents - risk-on equities, lower oil, lower yields, a softer dollar, and firmer gold - leave the macro market leaning toward easier financial conditions into the decision, but with the policy signal still unresolved.
