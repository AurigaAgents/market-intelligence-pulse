## Analysis

June 20 was a weekend non-session for every rates, FX and equity market that defines the macro tape, so there is no June 20 cash print to report. The standing macro picture is therefore date-staggered: the U.S. Treasury curve carries its June 18 close (Juneteenth on June 19 produced no print), while the dollar and Brent carry their Friday, June 19 closes. Oil and equity-index futures are shut until the Sunday electronic reopen around 18:00 ET. That left a single live macro channel over the weekend - geopolitical headline risk around the Strait of Hormuz. [C-2026-06-20-MACRO-C1-SESSION-STATUS]

U.S. rates stayed frozen at Thursday's close. The official Treasury par-yield CSV, read live on June 20, still ends at June 18 with the 2-year at 4.19%, the 10-year at 4.46% and the 30-year at 4.90% - a +27 bp 2s10s spread that had flattened from +29 bp on June 17. There is no June 19 row (Juneteenth) and no June 20 row (weekend), so the post-FOMC rates picture from earlier in the week was simply carried, neither extended nor unwound, into the long weekend. [C-2026-06-20-MACRO-C1-TREASURY-CARRY]

The dollar carried Friday's strength into the weekend. The U.S. Dollar Index sat around 100.8 at the June 19 close (Trading Economics spot 100.79, about -0.06% on the day; CNBC prior close 100.849), holding near a one-year / 13-month high after printing a fresh 52-week intraday high of 101.13 dated 06/19/26, and up roughly 1.1% on the week. The driver is squarely a macro-policy one: Trading Economics attributes the firm dollar to a hawkish Federal Reserve, with about half of FOMC members now projecting at least one 2026 rate hike, new Chair Kevin Warsh stressing that inflation has run above target for years, and markets pricing roughly a 50% chance of a 25 bp hike in September. With FX closed Saturday, that is a carried level, not a new move. [C-2026-06-20-MACRO-C1-DOLLAR-CARRY]

Oil closed the week soft and is now shut. Brent settled its last print at $80.59/bbl on June 19, up 0.93% on the day but down roughly 8% on the week, having spent the week giving back the Middle East risk premium as the interim U.S.-Iran deal reopened tanker flows. Because crude futures do not trade Saturday and reopen only Sunday evening ET, Friday's $80.59 is the standing reference and the weekend Hormuz headline is an unpriced gap risk into that reopen - skewed higher if the closure claim gains traction, capped by the U.S. assertion that traffic is still flowing. [C-2026-06-20-MACRO-C1-OIL-WEEKEND]

The new catalyst is the Strait of Hormuz itself. On Saturday, June 20, Iran's joint military command and the IRGC declared the strait closed again and warned vessels away, tying the move to continued Israeli operations in Lebanon and what Tehran called U.S. "bad faith" and a breach of the interim truce. The U.S. flatly denied it: CENTCOM spokesman Capt. Tim Hawkins said "Iran does not control the Strait of Hormuz," with the military reporting 55 merchant ships and more than 17 million barrels of oil transiting Saturday. The backdrop stayed violent - Israeli strikes in southern Lebanon killed at least 16 people on Saturday, the war's death toll passed 4,000, and a Hezbollah official said Iran will not reopen the strait until Israel publicly commits to a comprehensive ceasefire, even as an Israeli official said the military had received a cease-fire directive. So the chokepoint risk is real as a headline but contested as a physical disruption. [C-2026-06-20-MACRO-C1-HORMUZ-RECLOSURE]

That sets up Sunday. Technical-level U.S.-Iran talks are due to begin Sunday, June 21 in Switzerland, where Jared Kushner and Steve Witkoff were already working the details and an Iranian delegation (Speaker Qalibaf, FM Araghchi, plus central-bank and oil officials) was traveling to join; the parties have a 60-day window to convert Wednesday's memorandum of understanding into a final nuclear deal. President Trump used a Saturday Truth Social post to keep leverage on the table, pledging no Hormuz tolls during the 60-day ceasefire but threatening U.S. tolls afterward for "services rendered" if no final deal is reached. [C-2026-06-20-MACRO-C1-TALKS-SETUP]

Net read: the June 20 macro state is a quiet weekend carry over a loud geopolitical wire. Frozen U.S. rates, a dollar pinned near one-year highs by a hawkish Fed, and a Brent benchmark that just shed ~8% on the week all describe a market still leaning toward policy restraint and a fading-but-fragile Middle East risk premium. The Hormuz re-closure claim and the Sunday Switzerland talks are the two events that will decide whether the Sunday oil reopen gaps up or simply extends Friday's de-escalation trade.
