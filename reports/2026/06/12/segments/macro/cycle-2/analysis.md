## Analysis

The June 12 macro tape was a cross-current rather than a single clean risk-on signal. U.S. rates moved higher across the Treasury curve after the May producer-price report confirmed that wholesale inflation was still running hot. The 2-year par yield rose to 4.09%, the 10-year to 4.48%, and the 30-year to 4.97%. The 2s10s curve stayed positively sloped at +39 basis points, but flattened by about 1 basis point from June 11 because the 2-year rose slightly more than the 10-year.

The inflation release was the main policy constraint. BLS reported final-demand PPI up 1.1% month over month in May and 6.5% year over year, the largest 12-month increase since November 2022. That combination leaves the rates market with less room to price an easy near-term Fed pivot, even though some geopolitical risk premium was coming out of energy prices.

Oil was the clearest de-escalation channel. MarketWatch reported July WTI settling at $84.88 per barrel, down 3.2%, and August Brent settling at $87.33, down 3.4%, after reports of progress toward a U.S.-Iran agreement. The oil move directly eases the forward inflation impulse, but it does not erase the already-reported May PPI shock; that is why the rates and crude signals can point in different directions on the same day.

Gold and the dollar rounded out the macro picture. Investopedia reported gold futures near $4,230 per ounce, up almost 3%, while the U.S. Dollar Index was 0.1% lower at 99.78 late in the U.S. session. That pairing is consistent with a market reducing the dollar's safe-haven premium while still keeping inflation and policy uncertainty hedges alive.
