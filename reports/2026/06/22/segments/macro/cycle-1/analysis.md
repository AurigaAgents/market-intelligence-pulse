## Analysis

Monday, June 22, 2026 was the first full U.S. macro session after the three-day Juneteenth weekend, with cash Treasuries and equities reopening after the June 19 holiday and the June 20-21 weekend. That makes the day's rate and equity moves a comparison against Thursday, June 18: the official Treasury par-yield CSV, read live on June 23, has its newest row dated 06/22/2026 with no 06/19, 06/20 or 06/21 row in between. The session's macro tape was set by one dominant cross-asset driver - the conclusion of the first round of U.S.-Iran talks in Switzerland with a 60-day roadmap toward a final deal - layered on a still-hawkish Federal Reserve backdrop. The combination pulled crude sharply lower while leaving U.S. rates and the dollar firm. [C-2026-06-22-MACRO-C1-SESSION-STATUS]

U.S. Treasury yields backed up across the curve. The official par-yield curve closed June 22 at 4.24% on the 2-year, 4.25% on the 3-year, 4.29% on the 5-year, 4.39% on the 7-year, 4.51% on the 10-year, 4.97% on the 20-year and 4.95% on the 30-year - a roughly +5 to +6 bp parallel rise from the June 18 pre-holiday close (2Y 4.19%, 10Y 4.46%, 30Y 4.90%). The 2s10s slope was unchanged at +27 bp, so this was a bear shift rather than a steepening or flattening, and the long end kept its small kink, with the 20-year (4.97%) sitting 2 bp above the 30-year (4.95%). The back-up in yields is the macro reason equities carried a rates headwind on the day, weighing in particular on the rate-sensitive, mega-cap-heavy Nasdaq. [C-2026-06-22-MACRO-C1-TREASURY-CURVE]

The dollar stayed pinned near multi-year highs. The U.S. Dollar Index closed June 22 around 101.0 and pushed to roughly 101.17 on June 23 - a fresh high and its strongest level since May 2025. The driver is squarely monetary policy: with U.S. CPI back up at 4.2% (May) and the fed funds rate at 3.75%, markets are positioned for further Fed tightening rather than cuts, and both Deutsche Bank and BofA Global Research now carry a September rate hike in their forecasts after last week's hawkish Fed. This week's PCE print - the Fed's preferred inflation gauge - is the next catalyst. A dollar this firm, alongside rising yields, is the tightening-leaning half of the June 22 macro picture. [C-2026-06-22-MACRO-C1-DOLLAR]

Crude was the day's big mover, and it fell hard. Brent settled down 3.3% at $77.90 a barrel and WTI lost 2.3% to settle at $74.82, both around three-month lows. The path mattered: prices first spiked to about $82.30 early Monday on President Trump's threat to restart strikes on Iran and Tehran's renewed claim to have closed the Strait of Hormuz, then reversed lower through the session as Vice President JD Vance signaled "great progress" in the Switzerland talks and the strait kept flowing. A supply-side leg was added when the U.S. Treasury issued a 60-day license authorizing the production, delivery and sale of Iranian oil - including imports to the U.S., payable in dollars - reinforcing expectations of more barrels reaching the market; Brent is now down roughly 20% over the past month. [C-2026-06-22-MACRO-C1-OIL-SELLOFF]

The catalyst beneath the oil move was diplomatic. Mediators Qatar and Pakistan said the U.S. and Iran, meeting at the Burgenstock resort above Lake Lucerne, agreed on a roadmap aimed at a final deal within 60 days, building on the June 17 memorandum of understanding. The parties created a "High Level Committee" for political oversight, with working groups on nuclear, sanctions and dispute resolution, plus a U.S.-Iran-Lebanon "de-confliction" cell to end hostilities in Lebanon; technical talks were set to continue through the week. The MoU reopens the Strait of Hormuz toll-free for at least 60 days, and Iran's foreign minister Abbas Araghchi said Tehran had secured waivers for oil and petrochemical exports, a lifting of its port blockade, the release of some frozen assets and a reconstruction plan. For macro markets, this is the mechanism that converted a war-risk premium into a supply-normalization trade. [C-2026-06-22-MACRO-C1-IRAN-ROADMAP]

The de-escalation is real but fragile, which is why the oil move was a partial repricing rather than a collapse. The talks were described as "constructive but tense" and ran into the early hours; President Trump publicly threatened to "hit Iran very hard again" even as Vance negotiated, and Iran's IRGC had declared the Strait of Hormuz closed again on Saturday - a claim the U.S. military flatly denied ("Iran does not control the Strait of Hormuz"), with Iran's Tasnim agency saying the strait would stay shut until a Lebanon ceasefire and oil-sanctions relief. Lebanon remained the live flashpoint: Israeli strikes were reported to have killed at least 20 people on Saturday, and Araghchi called the new Lebanon de-confliction mechanism the agreement's "first real test." Analysts echoed the caution - ING flagged "very real risks" of a flare-up during the 60-day ceasefire, and Quantum Strategy's David Roche warned that the apparent abundance of Middle East oil reflects inventory and tanker liquidation rather than recovered production, leaving the market exposed once those stockpiles draw down. These items are evolving and are carried at medium confidence. [C-2026-06-22-MACRO-C1-FRAGILITY]

Net read: June 22's macro state was two-sided. The geopolitical channel turned decisively risk-positive - a 60-day U.S.-Iran roadmap and a U.S. license for Iranian oil drove Brent and WTI to three-month lows and lifted risk appetite, especially in AI-led Asian equities. But the monetary channel stayed restrictive - Treasury yields rose about 5 bp across the curve and the dollar held a fresh ~13-month high on a Fed the market still expects to hike in September. Lower oil is disinflationary at the margin and helps the Fed's task, but the immediate equity read was mixed: cheaper energy and firmer breadth (small caps, the Dow) against a rates-and-dollar headwind that hit the largest tech names. The two things to watch are this week's PCE print and whether the Lebanon de-confliction cell holds - the first decides the rates/dollar leg, the second decides whether the oil risk-premium unwind extends or snaps back.
