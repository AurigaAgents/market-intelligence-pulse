# US Segment Analysis - Cycle 1 (2026-06-17)

## Overview
US equities sold off broadly in the June 17, 2026 regular session as the Federal Reserve held rates steady but published a hawkish set of projections. The FOMC kept the federal funds target range at 3.50%-3.75% on a unanimous 12-0 vote, yet its updated Summary of Economic Projections lifted the median end-2026 policy-rate path to 3.8% from 3.4% in March, with nearly half of participants now penciling in at least one hike this year. The Dow erased an early 0.5% gain to finish down 1%, and all four major indexes closed lower while Treasury yields climbed.

## Drivers
The session was a single-catalyst day, and the catalyst was the Fed. The policy decision itself was a hold: the FOMC left the target range at 3.50%-3.75% and, unlike several recent meetings, did so unanimously (12-0). What moved the tape was the forward guidance embedded in the quarterly dot plot. The median participant now sees the appropriate end-2026 federal funds rate at 3.8%, a 0.4 percentage-point upward revision from March's 3.4%, and 9 of 18 participants placed their end-2026 midpoint at or above 3.875% - one quarter-point hike above the current 3.625% midpoint. The AP framed this as nearly half of policymakers foreseeing at least one increase this year, a clear reversal of the prior easing bias.

The projections also explain the why. The June SEP lifted the median 2026 PCE inflation forecast to 3.6% from 2.7% and core PCE to 3.3% from 2.7%, while the statement attributed elevated inflation in part to supply shocks "in certain sectors, including energy." That ties the hawkish turn directly to the energy-price channel of the Middle East conflict that has run through recent sessions. Growth and labor projections were little changed (2026 median GDP 2.2%, unemployment 4.3%), so this is an inflation-driven hawkish shift rather than a growth-downgrade story.

The reaction was textbook. Treasury yields climbed as markets priced in higher-for-longer policy, and equities de-rated: the Dow swung from a 0.5% intraday gain to a 1% loss, and breadth was negative across large caps, tech, and small caps alike. The implementation note confirmed the operating stance is unchanged - interest on reserve balances stays at 3.65% effective June 18, with the standing repo rate at 3.75%, the ON RRP offering rate at 3.5%, and the primary credit rate at 3.75%.

## Read-Through
For the US segment, the clean signal is "hawkish hold." The Fed did not raise rates, but it told the market that the bar to cutting has risen and the next move could be up - a message that lands harder because it is backed by a materially higher inflation projection rather than soft language. The week-to-date picture captures the whiplash: a record-setting Dow earlier in the week is now up just 0.6% for the week, the S&P 500 has slipped back to roughly flat (-0.2%), and the Russell 2000 has given back its weekly gains (-0.9%). Year-to-date gains remain positive but compressed from Tuesday's levels, with small caps (+17.6%) still leading and the Dow (+7.1%) the laggard. The risk to watch is the energy-inflation feedback loop: as long as the SEP's supply-shock framing holds, equity multiples sit under a higher-rate cloud, and rallies into a record Dow are vulnerable to projection-driven repricing like today's.
