## Overview
US equities sold off into the June 17, 2026 regular-session close after the Federal Reserve kept rates unchanged but published a June SEP with a materially more hawkish 2026 rate distribution. AP's closing ledger showed all four tracked US equity indexes lower on the day: the S&P 500 fell 1.2% to 7,420.10, the Dow fell 1.0% to 51,492.55 after being up 0.5% in the morning, the Nasdaq Composite fell 1.3% to 26,021.66, and the Russell 2000 fell 0.7% to 2,917.98.

## Drivers
The policy decision was not the shock; the path distribution was. The FOMC maintained the 3.50%-3.75% target range by a 12-0 vote, and the implementation note kept the main administered settings aligned with that range. The equity reaction instead tracks the new SEP: 9 of 18 participants projected a 2026 year-end federal-funds midpoint above the current 3.625% midpoint, versus 8 at the current midpoint and 1 below it.

The macro message left equities with less room to treat the hold as dovish. The statement said activity was expanding at a solid pace, job gains had kept pace with the workforce, and unemployment had changed little, while inflation remained elevated relative to the 2% goal. The SEP reinforced that tension with 2026 medians of 2.2% real GDP growth, 4.3% unemployment, 3.6% PCE inflation, and 3.3% core PCE inflation.

AP also reported Treasury yields climbed on rising expectations for a 2026 hike. That rates response fits the cross-asset signal: higher expected policy rates pressured equity valuations, and the Dow's move from a morning gain to a 1.0% close-to-close loss shows the weakness reached blue chips as well as the Nasdaq.

## Read-Through
Cycle 2 replaces the blocked cycle-1 US segment with a cleaner source trail and a narrower conclusion. The June 17 tape was a Fed-driven selloff, not a headline-level milestone story. The S&P 500 and Russell 2000 were negative week to date through the close, while the Dow and Nasdaq still held modest week-to-date gains, so the near-term damage was sharp but uneven.

The key US read-through is that the market repriced the policy reaction function. A same-range Fed hold would normally be steadying; a dot distribution with half of SEP participants above the current midpoint by year-end made that hold conditional. Until inflation data cools enough to challenge the 3.6% PCE and 3.3% core PCE 2026 medians, US equities are vulnerable to yield-led valuation pressure even when growth and labor language remain firm.
