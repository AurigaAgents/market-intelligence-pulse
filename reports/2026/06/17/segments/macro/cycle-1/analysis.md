## Analysis

June 17 was a policy-driven repricing, and the impulse ran in the opposite direction to the prior session's energy-led de-risking. The Federal Reserve left its federal funds target range unchanged at 3.50%-3.75% — the widely expected outcome — but did so in a unanimous 12-0 vote with no dovish dissenters at Kevin Warsh's first meeting as Chair. The hold was never the story; the projections and the language were. [C-2026-06-17-MACRO-C1-006]

The hawkish signal came through the dot plot and the statement together. The June Summary of Economic Projections lifted the median year-end 2026 federal funds rate to 3.8% from 3.4% in March, with nine officials now penciling in at least one rate hike by year-end, and the post-meeting statement stripped out prior language that had flagged the likelihood of rate cuts this year. Notably, Warsh himself was the only one of 19 officials who did not submit a rate-path projection, and in the press conference he pushed against forward guidance — describing the dots as written in pencil "with big erasers" and launching five task forces to review communications, the balance sheet, and other policy machinery. The result is a committee that tilted decidedly more hawkish even as the new Chair tried to lower the weight markets place on its forecasts. [C-2026-06-17-MACRO-C1-007, C-2026-06-17-MACRO-C1-008]

Rates repriced immediately and in textbook fashion for a hawkish surprise. On the official Treasury par curve the 2-year yield jumped 15 basis points to 4.20% — the largest single-day move on the June 2026 curve — while the 10-year rose only 6 basis points to 4.49% and the 30-year was unchanged at 4.93%. Because the front end sold off far harder than the long end, the 2s10s par spread bear-flattened 9 basis points to +29 basis points. In real-time market quotes the move was even more vivid: the 2-year yield climbed roughly 16-17 basis points to 4.216%, its highest since February 2025, with the heaviest selling in the final hour. A bear flattening of this shape is the market saying the same thing as the dots — higher-for-longer, with the policy rate biased up rather than down. [C-2026-06-17-MACRO-C1-001, C-2026-06-17-MACRO-C1-002, C-2026-06-17-MACRO-C1-003, C-2026-06-17-MACRO-C1-004, C-2026-06-17-MACRO-C1-005]

The rate path repriced with it. After the decision, fed funds futures priced roughly a 72% chance of a hike by October and cut the odds of rates simply holding through year-end to about 13%, down from 40% the day before, with a September hike now seen as more likely than a hold. That is a wholesale shift from a market that had been debating the timing of cuts only weeks earlier to one fully pricing a hike before year-end. [C-2026-06-17-MACRO-C1-009]

Equities took the hawkish turn poorly across the board. The S&P 500 fell 1.21% to 7,420.10 and the Nasdaq Composite dropped 1.34% to 26,021.66, both selling off sharply in the last hour, while the Dow swung from a morning gain of about 0.5% to a 0.98% loss at 51,492.55, snapping a two-session run of record closes. Breadth confirmed the macro nature of the move: all 11 S&P 500 sectors closed lower, communication services worst at roughly -3% and industrials most resilient at -0.1%, the small-cap Russell 2000 fell 0.7% to 2,917.98, and the VIX rose 2 points to 18.44. When every sector falls together on a Fed day, the driver is the discount rate, not any single industry. [C-2026-06-17-MACRO-C1-010, C-2026-06-17-MACRO-C1-011, C-2026-06-17-MACRO-C1-012, C-2026-06-17-MACRO-C1-013]

Cross-asset confirmation rounded out the picture. The dollar index rose 0.9% to 100.47 as higher yields and the hawkish shift pulled flows into the currency. Crude was the one quiet corner: WTI was essentially flat at about $76.10 a barrel, edging up only slightly after President Trump said the U.S.-Iran agreement was not final and the war could resume, having already slid earlier in the week on the preliminary peace deal. That stabilization matters for the macro read, because it removes the energy disinflation tailwind the Fed had been handed and leaves domestic demand as the swing factor. And demand is firm: May retail sales rose 0.9%, nearly double the 0.5% consensus and up 6.9% year over year, a fourth straight strong month, with service-station receipts up 3.4% on higher gasoline prices. [C-2026-06-17-MACRO-C1-014, C-2026-06-17-MACRO-C1-015, C-2026-06-17-MACRO-C1-016]

Net read: this was the mirror image of June 16. Where the prior session eased financial conditions through falling oil, lower yields, and a flatter bull curve, June 17 tightened them through a hawkish Fed, a front-end-led yield spike, a stronger dollar, and a broad equity sell-off. The unifying thread across both days is inflation: resilient retail demand and the fading of the post-Iran energy relief have moved the FOMC's central tendency from "when do we cut" to "do we hike," and the bond market has already taken the committee at its word.
