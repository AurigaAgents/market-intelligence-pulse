## Analysis

US equities split sharply along style and sector lines on Tuesday, June 16, 2026, the session after Monday's U.S.-Iran/Hormuz de-escalation rally. The blue-chip **Dow Jones Industrial Average** rose 328.64 points (+0.6%) to a record **51,999.67** — its second straight record close — while the broad **S&P 500** slipped 42.94 points (−0.6%) to **7,511.35** and the tech-heavy **Nasdaq Composite** fell 307.60 points (−1.2%) to **26,376.34**. The small-cap **Russell 2000** declined 25.89 points (−0.9%) to **2,939.19**. The leadership ranking was the inverse of Monday's risk-on tape: the cyclically weighted Dow out front, the growth-heavy Nasdaq at the back.

The divergence was an AI-stock story. Drops in several influential AI names weighed on the market; after leading both the advance and the recent swings, those shares pulled back on worries their prices had risen too far. Within the S&P 500 advancers and decliners were nearly evenly split, so the index's modest loss masked a market that was rotating rather than broadly selling off. Mega-caps were mixed — Alphabet, Apple, Amazon and Meta firmed while Nvidia, Microsoft and Tesla each fell more than 1% in intraday trade. The Dow's record was carried instead by financials and industrials: JPMorgan Chase rose about 3% to lead the average, and Caterpillar — increasingly treated as an AI-power-demand play — traded at a record high, helping lift the industrial sector back above its pre-conflict levels.

The macro backdrop reinforced the rotation. **Brent crude fell below $80 per barrel for the first time since early March** as optimism over the tentative U.S.-Iran deal and the planned reopening of the Strait of Hormuz continued to drain the war-risk premium; Investopedia put Brent near $78.90 (down more than 5%) and U.S. WTI near $76 in intraday trade, with the 10-year Treasury yield easing to 4.43% from 4.48%. The Federal Reserve opened its two-day policy meeting — new Chair Kevin Warsh's first — with a rate decision due Wednesday and markets widely expecting no change, while President Trump met G7 leaders in France with the Hormuz timeline in focus. Cheaper energy and lower yields favored rate-sensitive cyclicals over crowded tech.

Beneath the day's moves the year-to-date picture stayed firmly positive: the **S&P 500** is up 9.7%, the **Dow** +8.2%, the **Nasdaq** +13.5%, and the **Russell 2000** +18.4% — small caps remaining the YTD leader among the major U.S. indexes even after Tuesday's pullback. The session reads as healthy consolidation: a one-day breather in stretched AI leadership absorbed by a rotation into financials and industrials that pushed the Dow to a fresh high, rather than a broad de-risking.
