Market Intelligence Pulse - 2026-06-24

Summary

June 24 was a fragile stabilization after Tuesday's global technology rout. The split was everywhere: the Dow rose 182.06 points, or 0.4%, to 51,848.90 and the Russell 2000 gained 11.15 points, or 0.4%, to 2,986.63, while the S&P 500 fell 7.24 points, or 0.1%, to 7,358.22 and the Nasdaq Composite dropped 110.40 points, or 0.4%, to 25,476.64. Nearly two out of every three stocks in the S&P 500 gained ground despite the index decline, because Microsoft (-2.3%) and Oracle (-4.6%) alone dragged the cap-weighted composite negative. [C-2026-06-24-US-C2-EQUITY-CLOSE, C-2026-06-24-US-C2-BREADTH, C-2026-06-24-US-C2-TECH-DRAG]

Asia set the stabilization tone first. South Korea's KOSPI surged 267.18 points, or 3.26%, to 8,471.02 on bargain hunting after Tuesday's roughly 10% crash, while Japan's Nikkei 225 fell for a second consecutive day, down 613.41 points, or 0.88%, to 69,174.97. Greater China was modestly higher: the Hang Seng rose 0.33% to 23,412.18 and the Shanghai Composite added 0.11% to 4,110.81. [C-2026-06-24-ASIA-C1-REGIONAL-CLOSE, C-2026-06-24-ASIA-C1-KOSPI-REBOUND, C-2026-06-24-ASIA-C1-NIKKEI-DECLINE, C-2026-06-24-ASIA-C1-GREATER-CHINA]

The macro tape was dominated by crude oil's slide to a four-month low. Brent fell near $73.97/bbl, down about 4% on the day, as US-Iran de-escalation and the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz eased supply fears. The oil drop pulled Treasury yields lower across the curve — 2Y to 4.11% and 10Y to 4.41%, a bull flattening that narrowed the 2s10s to +30 bp — and dragged market inflation expectations to the year's lows. But the dollar still rose for a sixth straight session to a 13-month high near 101.6, as the market prices a second 2026 Fed hike ahead of Thursday's May PCE print (consensus +4.1% YoY headline). [C-2026-06-24-MACRO-C1-OIL, C-2026-06-24-MACRO-C1-TREASURY-CURVE, C-2026-06-24-MACRO-C1-DOLLAR, C-2026-06-24-MACRO-C1-FED-PCE]

Europe was the most idiosyncratic tape. The STOXX Europe 600 edged up 0.08% to 635.16 and the CAC 40 rose 0.54% to 8,385.49 (snapping a three-session losing streak), while the DAX fell as much as 0.73% to 24,712.70, dragged by Rheinmetall's roughly -18.7% collapse after Germany scrapped its F126 frigate programme. The FTSE 100 added 0.31% to 10,461.63. Segro surged 17.4% on the Prologis bid, the real estate sector rose 3%, and defence, miners and energy lagged. [C-2026-06-24-EU-C1-INDEX-CLOSE, C-2026-06-24-EU-C1-MOVERS, C-2026-06-24-EU-C1-SECTOR-ROTATION]

Sentiment shifted from June 23's unambiguous risk-off to "fragile stabilization, tech still bleeding." The VIX eased 4.41% to 18.63 with the VIX9D-to-VIX spread restoring a normal upward slope, but the Fear & Greed Index stayed in fear at 27-28 and the S&P 500 and Nasdaq extended their losing streak to three sessions. AAII and flow readings remain pre-selloff context. [C-2026-06-24-SENTIMENT-C1-VOLATILITY, C-2026-06-24-SENTIMENT-C1-FEAR-GREED, C-2026-06-24-SENTIMENT-C1-SYNTHESIS]

C-2026-06-24-MACRO-C1-INFLATION-EXPECTATIONS

The mechanism behind the yield drop was collapsing market-based inflation expectations. Reuters reported the US 5-year breakeven inflation rate at 2.20%, its lowest this year, with the 10-year breakeven below that at its lowest since April 2025; one-year euro-area inflation swaps fell back below the ECB's 2% target and the 2-year UK inflation swap hit a six-month low. Falling energy prices as the Middle East conflict cools are pulling forward-looking inflation pricing down rapidly - which is why nominal Treasury yields fell even though realized PCE inflation is expected to print a three-year high on Thursday.
