## Overview
Asia-Pacific equities were mixed on Wednesday, June 24, 2026, as markets attempted to stabilize after a brutal global technology selloff the prior session. The clearest pattern: bargain-hunting lifted markets that had been hammered on Tuesday, while Japanese equities fell for a second consecutive day. South Korea's KOSPI surged 267.18 points, or 3.26%, to close at 8,471.02, rebounding from a roughly 10% crash on Tuesday — its sharpest one-day drop since March. Japan's Nikkei 225 declined 613.41 points, or 0.88%, to 69,174.97 in volatile trading. Hong Kong's Hang Seng rose 75.90 points, or 0.33%, to 23,412.18, and the Shanghai Composite added 0.11% to 4,110.81.

The session should not be read as a clean risk-on rebound. MSCI's index of Asian equities outside Japan was roughly flat (Reuters reported +0.04%; a Reuters intraday wrap via Business Standard earlier showed -0.02%). The KOSPI's 3.26% gain came through choppy, two-way trading: the index briefly fell into negative territory on heavy foreign selling and neared 8,000 before retail and institutional buyers turned it around. Foreign investors sold a net 4.66 trillion won, while retail and institutions purchased 2.63 trillion and 1.92 trillion won respectively. Analysts cautioned that the rapid two-way price action was itself a sign of instability, not resolution.

## Session Status
Wednesday, June 24, 2026 was a regular cash-equity trading day across all referenced Asian markets. There were no market holidays. The session context is critical: Tuesday, June 23 saw a global technology and semiconductor selloff. Reuters reported the KOSPI plunged roughly 10% on Tuesday — its sharpest one-day drop since March — while the S&P 500 fell 1.4% and the Nasdaq Composite fell 2.2% on Wall Street overnight. June 24 was therefore the first stabilization test after that rout, and the regional pattern reflects it: the most heavily sold-down market (Korea) rebounded hardest, while Japan continued to slide.

## Index Closes
The regional close picture was sourced primarily from MarketWatch Automation, backed by Dow Jones/FactSet market data. MarketWatch reported the Hang Seng Index up 0.33% at 23,412.18, the Nikkei 225 down 0.88% to 69,174.97, the Shanghai Composite up 0.11% to 4,110.81, the KOSPI up 3.3% to 8,471.02, the S&P/ASX 200 up 0.24% to 8,808.40, and the FTSE Straits Times up 0.20% to 5,216.44.

The Nikkei close is independently confirmed by WSJ market data, which showed the index at 69,174.97, down 613.41 points (-0.88%) as of 3:45 PM JST, with an intraday range from 68,461.10 to 70,218.71. The previous close was 69,788.38. The wide range — over 1,750 points between the intraday low and high — underscores the session's volatility.

The KOSPI close is anchored by Yonhap's close bulletin: the benchmark gained 267.18 points, or 3.26%, to close at 8,471.02. Reuters independently confirmed the +3.26% figure and the prior-session 10% crash context. The rebound was not smooth: the index opened higher, briefly fell into negative territory on heavy foreign selling, and dropped close to the 8,000-point level before strong retail and institutional buying reversed the course. Samsung Electronics surged 9.84% and SK hynix rose 0.98%.

For Greater China, MarketWatch and AP both put the Hang Seng at 23,412.18 (+0.3%) and the Shanghai Composite at 4,110.81 (+0.1%). MarketWatch listed semiconductor and biotech stocks among the top Hang Seng gainers: Semicon Manufacturing Intl +8.93%, WuXi AppTec +8.33%, and Wuxi Biologics +4.17%.

## Session Drivers
The macro backdrop was dominated by the aftermath of Tuesday's technology selloff and shifting expectations around US monetary policy. A Reuters article carried by Business Standard quoted Michael McCarthy, market analyst at Moomoo Securities Australia: "Price action in markets over the last seven trading days has been alarming, not just when it falls, but also when it rises. When markets move so rapidly, in either direction, it's a sign of instability."

On the macro side, oil prices extended losses toward four-month lows on signs that more tankers stranded in the Gulf were set to move through the Strait of Hormuz as US-Iran negotiations progressed. Reuters reported Brent settled at $73.74 per barrel, down 4.33% on the day. AP separately reported Brent down 3.8% to $73.87 and US crude down 3.9% to $70.34. The conflicting accounts of the US-Iran deal — including nuclear inspections and control of the strait — kept uncertainty high.

The dollar strengthened for a third straight day, with the dollar index rising 0.24% to 101.63, its highest level since May 2025. The yen traded near 40-year lows around 161.81 per dollar, keeping markets on edge over potential currency intervention. A summary of the Bank of Japan's June meeting, released Wednesday, showed some board members called for further rate hikes after the central bank raised its policy rate to a 31-year high of 1.00%.

Looking ahead, two risk events shaped cautious positioning: Micron Technology's earnings release after the US close on Wednesday (a key read on AI-driven memory chip demand), and the US PCE inflation data due Thursday, with economists expecting prices to have risen 4.1% in May — which would be the highest level in three years.
