## Analysis

Wednesday, June 24, 2026 was a normal global macro session and the first full day after the June 23 technology-led equity selloff. The official Treasury par-yield CSV, read live, has its newest row dated 06/24/2026 directly after the 06/23/2026 row, so the day's rate moves are a comparison against Tuesday, June 23. The session's macro tape was set by one dominant cross-asset driver - crude oil's slide to a four-month low as US-Iran de-escalation and the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz eased supply fears - which pulled US Treasury yields and market inflation expectations lower across the board, against a still-hawkish Federal Reserve and a dollar at a 13-month high. [C-2026-06-24-MACRO-C1-SESSION-STATUS]

US Treasury yields tumbled across the curve. The official par-yield curve closed June 24 at 4.11% on the 2-year, 4.15% on the 3-year, 4.17% on the 5-year, 4.28% on the 7-year, 4.41% on the 10-year, 4.87% on the 20-year and 4.86% on the 30-year - down roughly 5 to 10 basis points from the June 23 close (2Y 4.16%, 10Y 4.50%, 30Y 4.94%), with the belly and long end leading. This was a bull flattening: the 2s10s slope on the official constant-maturity basis narrowed to +30 bp from +34 bp, and Reuters put the market-based 2s/10s near 25 bp, its flattest since March 2025. The long end kept its small kink, with the 20-year (4.87%) one basis point above the 30-year (4.86%). Falling yields were the day's macro relief signal. [C-2026-06-24-MACRO-C1-TREASURY-CURVE]

The mechanism beneath the yield drop was a sharp repricing of inflation expectations. Reuters reported the US 5-year breakeven inflation rate at 2.20%, its lowest this year, with the 10-year breakeven below that at its lowest since April 2025; one-year euro-area inflation swaps fell back below the ECB's 2% target and the 2-year UK inflation swap hit a six-month low. As the Middle East conflict cools, supply routes reopen and energy prices tumble, forward-looking inflation pricing is falling rapidly - which is exactly why nominal Treasury yields could fall even though realized PCE inflation is set to print a three-year high on Thursday. [C-2026-06-24-MACRO-C1-INFLATION-EXPECTATIONS]

Crude oil was the engine of the move, and it fell hard. Brent traded near $73.97 a barrel on June 24, down about 4% on the day and at its lowest since late February - a four-month low; the Associated Press put the Brent close near $73.87 and Reuters flagged the lowest level since the Iran war began, with WTI near $70. Brent is down roughly 23% over the past month and about 40% from its wartime peak. The driver was supply normalization: more tankers moving out of the Strait of Hormuz after US-Iran de-escalation, President Trump's statement that Iran had told Washington no transit tolls were being sought, and the IEA's estimate that the UAE is exporting at about 85% of pre-war levels. The narrative held even though US EIA crude inventories sit at their lowest since 1984. Oil's decline is the disinflationary impulse that pulled yields and breakevens down. [C-2026-06-24-MACRO-C1-OIL]

The dollar, by contrast, ground higher. Reuters reported the dollar index rose for a sixth straight session to a 13-month high, driven by hawkish Fed repricing and safe-haven demand during the week's tech rout. Trading Economics' ICE DXY basis showed the index at 101.13 at the June 23 close and near 101.1 in early June 24 trade, with the June 24 close reported around 101.6 - the precise level carried at medium confidence given vendor and snapshot differences. A dollar this firm is the tightening-leaning half of the macro picture, but Reuters made a notable point: the surge is causing less global consternation than usual precisely because tumbling oil and energy prices are offsetting its inflationary bite on the rest of the world. [C-2026-06-24-MACRO-C1-DOLLAR]

The monetary backdrop stayed restrictive, which is what makes the day two-sided. After last week's hawkish Fed statement and Chair Kevin Warsh's press conference, traders added to bets of a second 25 bp rate hike by the end of December on CME's FedWatch tool, up from a single rise previously. The next catalyst is Thursday's May PCE price index (released June 25, 8:30 a.m. EDT), the Fed's preferred inflation gauge, which economists expect to rise 4.1% year over year - the highest since April 2023 and up from 3.8% in April, with the BEA's official series having climbed from 2.9% in January to 3.8% in April - alongside core PCE near 3.3%. Crucially, economists widely expect May to mark this year's inflation peak, because the very oil decline driving markets today should curb price pressures in the months ahead. [C-2026-06-24-MACRO-C1-FED-PCE]

The tightening bias was global. The Bank of Japan's Summary of Opinions from its June 15-16 meeting, released Wednesday, affirmed its rate-hike stance: at that meeting the BOJ lifted its policy rate 25 bp to 1.0% in a 7-1 vote (board member Asada Toichiro dissenting) - the highest since September 1995 and its first hike since December - and one member said it is appropriate to keep raising the rate as underlying inflation approaches 2%, with some members calling for a faster pace. There is a quiet irony here: the BOJ's stated rationale was capping an Iran-war energy shock that the same day's oil collapse is busy undoing. Even so, the yen languished near multi-decade lows against the 13-month-high dollar, a textbook rate-differential and dollar-strength story. [C-2026-06-24-MACRO-C1-BOJ]

Precious metals were the casualty of the strong dollar and hawkish Fed. Gold hovered around the $4,000-an-ounce mark - Reuters said spot slipped below $4,000 to its lowest this year, while the COMEX future held just above at about $4,018.80 (+0.25% on MarketWatch), a disclosed spot-versus-future basis gap - and is down roughly 12% in June, its worst month since 2008. Silver fell about 8% on the day and is now down some 55% from its January peak. The combination of the 13-month-high dollar, repricing toward a second Fed hike, and the fading Middle East safe-haven bid drove the metals lower; the VIX, by contrast, fell to 18.63 (down 4.4%), consistent with the day's broader risk stabilization after the tech rout. These daily metals moves are carried at medium confidence. [C-2026-06-24-MACRO-C1-GOLD-SILVER]

Net read: June 24's macro state was a one-way disinflation impulse colliding with a hawkish policy backdrop. Crude's slide to a four-month low pulled Treasury yields down across the curve, flattened the 2s/10s to its lowest since March 2025, dragged market inflation expectations to the year's lows, and sent gold below $4,000 - while the same hawkish-Fed repricing that has the market pricing a second 2026 hike kept the dollar grinding to a 13-month high and the BOJ affirming further tightening. The whole disinflation trade rests on Middle East de-escalation holding: continued Strait of Hormuz tanker flows and Trump's statement that Iran seeks no tolls. A flare-up would snap oil and the risk premium back; absent that, Thursday's May PCE print - expected at a three-year-high 4.1% - decides whether the front end can keep pace with the falling long end. The two things to watch are the PCE release and whether the tanker-flow normalization through Hormuz sticks. [C-2026-06-24-MACRO-C1-SYNTHESIS]
