## Analysis

The June 23 macro tape was a risk-off rotation caused by equity concentration stress, not a broad rates shock. Trading Economics described U.S. stock indices as negative after heavyweight chip producers sold off on rising skepticism that hyperscaler AI-infrastructure spending will generate adequate returns; its U.S. stock market page put the US500 at 7,367, down 1.42%, and said the S&P 500 fell 1.4% while the Nasdaq 100 lost 3.3%. That equity driver is important for the macro read because the same source notes Treasury yields remained elevated after the prior week's hawkish dot plot, yet the official Treasury close shows front-end yields falling on the day. The result was defensive cross-asset behavior: lower front-end yields, higher volatility, a firmer dollar, and weaker crude. [C-2026-06-23-MACRO-C1-SESSION-BASIS] [C-2026-06-23-MACRO-C1-EQUITY-RISK]

Treasuries rallied, led by the front end. The official par-yield curve closed June 23 at 4.16% on the 2-year, 4.22% on the 3-year, 4.27% on the 5-year, 4.38% on the 7-year, 4.50% on the 10-year, 4.96% on the 20-year, and 4.94% on the 30-year. Against June 22, the 2-year fell 8 bp while the 10-year and 30-year eased only 1 bp each. That widened 2s10s from +27 bp to +34 bp. In plain terms, the market bought the front end while leaving long-duration compensation mostly intact; that is consistent with a safe-haven bid and some trimming of near-term hike risk during a sharp equity selloff, rather than a full dovish repricing of the long end. [C-2026-06-23-MACRO-C1-TREASURY-CURVE]

The dollar moved the other way from front-end yields, which makes the safety channel visible. DXY rose to 101.3749 on June 23, up 0.35% on the day and 2.15% over the prior month. Trading Economics described the move as a fourth consecutive advance to around 101.3, the highest since April last year, supported by prospective Fed hikes, flight-to-safety demand triggered by the technology-stock selloff, stronger-than-expected flash S&P Global PMI data, and focus on the week's PCE inflation report. The combination of firmer DXY and lower 2-year yields says investors were not simply buying dollars because U.S. yields rose; they were also paying for liquidity and safety during an equity concentration shock. [C-2026-06-23-MACRO-C1-DOLLAR]

Crude kept moving lower, giving the day a disinflationary offset. Brent fell to $76.77/bbl, down 1.45% on the day and 20.58% over the month, while WTI fell to $72.95/bbl, down 1.23% on the day and 22.30% over the month. Trading Economics said both benchmarks were near their lowest levels in almost three months as traders monitored progress toward resolving the conflict with Iran. The public source narrative tied the move to a new 60-day U.S. waiver permitting global buyers, including American refineries, to purchase Iranian crude and fuels, plus working groups on nuclear and sanctions issues and rising Persian Gulf supply. The caveat is equally important: the same pages flagged unresolved discrepancies around negotiations, nuclear-inspection claims, Lebanon, and the Strait of Hormuz. Lower oil helps the inflation impulse at the margin, but it is not yet a durable peace dividend. [C-2026-06-23-MACRO-C1-OIL]

Volatility confirmed the equity stress. Trading Economics showed USVIX at 19.49 on June 23, up 2.21 points from the previous trading session, implying a prior-session level of 17.28. That is a meaningful risk-off move, but not a panic regime: it is volatility rising from a still-contained base as investors de-risked the crowded AI and semiconductor complex. The equity selloff was concentrated enough to hurt the Nasdaq 100 sharply, while healthcare and consumer-defensive gains helped the Dow stay near flat. For macro, the relevant read is concentration fragility: the market is still willing to own defensives and safe havens, but it is less willing to underwrite long-duration AI cash-flow assumptions at the same valuation. [C-2026-06-23-MACRO-C1-VOLATILITY] [C-2026-06-23-MACRO-C1-EQUITY-RISK]

Net read: June 23 was a two-channel macro session. The risk channel tightened through equities and volatility, with the chip/AI unwind pressuring the broad U.S. tape and lifting USVIX. The inflation channel eased through oil, where US-Iran supply-normalization headlines pushed Brent and WTI to nearly three-month lows. Rates split the difference: the front end rallied as risk-off challenged near-term hike conviction, but the long end barely moved, and the dollar stayed strong on safety demand plus a still-hawkish policy backdrop. The next macro test is whether cheaper energy and the coming PCE data can soften the Fed path enough to offset concentration stress in AI-linked equities; until then, the June 23 setup is defensive, dollar-supportive, and oil-disinflationary, with long-end rates still resistant to a full rally.
