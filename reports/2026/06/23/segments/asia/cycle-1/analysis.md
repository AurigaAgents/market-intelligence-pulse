## Overview
Asian equities suffered a brutal, technology-led selloff on Tuesday, June 23, 2026, with South Korea's KOSPI at the epicenter. The KOSPI crashed 9.99% to close at 8,203.84 — its steepest one-day decline since early March — wiping out the record high set just one session earlier. Japan's Nikkei 225 fell 3.6% to 69,788.38 and the TOPIX dropped 2.6% to 3,990, both retreating from all-time highs. Hong Kong's Hang Seng shed 2.1% to 23,263.41 and the Shanghai Composite dropped 1.4% to 4,106.25. The trigger was a coordinated unwinding of crowded AI and semiconductor trades that had powered the region to records into June, amplified by leveraged exchange-traded funds and a hawkish Federal Reserve overhang. This was a normal regional trading session, so the segment carries verified closes plus the day's drivers, not a no-session holiday read.

## Session Drivers
The selloff originated in overnight US weakness where the Nasdaq fell more than 1% as SpaceX shares tumbled and market giants Amazon, Nvidia and Microsoft came under pressure. That set the tone for Asia at the open. AFP framed the regional rout around doubts over the wisdom of the vast sums being pumped into artificial intelligence with little sign of returns, and pointed to SpaceX's 16% plunge — wiping roughly $600 billion in market value over three days — as the day's main corporate catalyst. Reuters, via Investing.com, highlighted a combination of profit-taking after outsized AI-driven gains, overnight US tech weakness, and a hawkish Federal Reserve meeting that had scaled back rate-cut expectations. The decline was not random: it was a synchronized repricing of the most crowded AI and semiconductor positions across the region after weeks of parabolic gains.

## South Korea: KOSPI Ground Zero
South Korea bore the brunt because its benchmark is the most concentrated in the very chipmakers that had led the rally. The KOSPI crashed 9.99% to 8,203.84 at the close, its steepest one-day drop since early March, after setting a record high just one session earlier. Bloomberg reported that the decline had extended to 9.1% as trading resumed after a 20-minute Korea Exchange circuit breaker suspension, before worsening further into the close. SK Hynix slumped more than 12% and Samsung Electronics lost more than 10%, together dragging the index lower given their combined weight approaching half the KOSPI's market capitalization. Foreign investors sold more than 4 trillion won of KOSPI shares while retail traders added positions. Bloomberg highlighted that leveraged ETFs tied to the chipmakers had amplified price swings, prompting the top financial watchdog to express regret for having allowed the risky products. Joo Won, head of the economic research division at Hyundai Research Institute, told AFP the scale of the decline appeared excessive and warned that "there may still be considerable selling pressure waiting in the wings." Gary Tan, portfolio manager at Allspring Global Investments, said the Korean market had become "increasingly sensitive to shifts in retail investor sentiment, particularly as attention has turned to leverage in the retail base."

## Japan: Record Run Halted
Japan's record-breaking rally hit a wall. The Nikkei 225 fell 3.6% to close at 69,788.38 and the broader TOPIX dropped 2.6% to 3,990 per Trading Economics close-level readings, both benchmarks pulling back from all-time highs set earlier in the week. The decline was concentrated in technology: SoftBank Group slumped 10.1% and Kioxia plummeted 15.1%, while chip-equipment maker Tokyo Electron shed 6.2% and PCB maker Ibiden dropped 6.6%. Reuters noted that Japanese manufacturing and services PMI data had both picked up in June, though input prices rose sharply due to disruptions tied to the Iran conflict. The yen remained a macro pressure point, sitting just below a 40-year low against the dollar near 161.93 despite more than $70 billion of intervention spending by Japanese authorities in the prior month. NHK and Kyodo News reported that Finance Minister Satsuki Katayama and US Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent had spoken about exchange rates, which helped the yen recover slightly from its low.

## Regional Breadth
The selloff was not confined to Seoul and Tokyo. AFP reported that Hong Kong's Hang Seng Index fell 2.1% to 23,263.41, the Shanghai Composite dropped 1.4% to 4,106.25, and Taipei also suffered strong selling pressure. Sydney, Singapore, Wellington, Mumbai, Bangkok and Jakarta were all sharply down. The Reuters early-session wrap, published before the regional selling intensified, had shown a more mixed picture — the S&P/ASX 200 roughly flat and the Nifty slightly higher — but those intraday readings were superseded by the final broad-based decline. The early-session intraday readings are carried at medium confidence and are consistent with a market that started cautiously and deteriorated through the day as overnight US losses and the Korean circuit breaker deepened risk aversion.

## Macro Backdrop: Oil, Iran, and the Fed
Oil extended losses on optimism over US-Iran peace negotiations. AFP reported WTI down 1.5% to $72.77 a barrel and Brent down 1.6% to $76.65, after the US Treasury temporarily lifted sanctions on Iran to allow crude sales up to August 21. US Vice President JD Vance said a "very good foundation" had been laid for negotiations toward a final deal. The Strait of Hormuz saw an uptick in tanker traffic after Iran reopened it. The macro overhang for Asian risk assets remained: the prior week's hawkish Federal Reserve meeting had prompted traders to scale back rate-cut expectations, and a Bank of America research note (cited in the US segment of this report) had flagged no rate cuts until 2028 and as many as three hikes this year. That combination — easier oil supply but tighter-for-longer monetary policy — created an awkward cross-current for Asian equities that had rallied on the AI/chip narrative rather than on macro fundamentals.

## AI and Valuation Crossroads
The day's narrative was a reckoning for the AI trade that had carried Asian benchmarks to records. AFP reported that investors were questioning both the long-running AI-fueled boom and the soaring price of SpaceX shares after its IPO, with SpaceX plunging about 16% and reportedly losing $600 billion in market value over three days. IG's Tony Sycamore, quoted by AFP, wrote that "valuations have become stretched and the bar is now materially higher" and that "questions around capital expenditure and returns on artificial intelligence spending remain unanswered." Reuters noted that the KOSPI had declined only twice in the previous seven sessions, leaving the benchmark vulnerable to a pullback as sentiment toward technology shares cooled. The proximate trigger, per TheStreet's live blog of the US session, was Broadcom's results failing to deliver the guidance boost markets expected — the same catalyst that had sparked the prior KOSPI crash on June 8.
