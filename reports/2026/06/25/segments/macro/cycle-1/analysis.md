## Analysis

Thursday, June 25, 2026 was a normal global macro session, but a busy one, framed by two events that pulled in opposite directions: the 8:30 a.m. EDT release of the May PCE price index and a renewed Strait of Hormuz supply scare after a cargo ship was struck off the Omani coast. The official Treasury par-yield CSV, read live, has its newest row dated 06/25/2026 directly after the 06/24/2026 row, so the day's rate moves are a comparison against Wednesday, June 24. The net result was two-sided and partly reversed the prior session: realized inflation confirmed firm, yet the in-line monthly print let Treasuries rally, while the geopolitical risk premium that June 24's oil collapse had defused came back into oil and gold. [C-2026-06-25-MACRO-C1-SESSION-STATUS]

US Treasury yields eased modestly. The official par-yield curve closed June 25 at 4.09% on the 2-year, 4.13% (3Y), 4.15% (5Y), 4.26% (7Y), 4.40% on the 10-year, 4.87% on the 20-year and 4.86% on the 30-year - down about 1 to 2 basis points across the front and belly versus the June 24 close (2Y 4.11%, 10Y 4.41%) and flat at the long end. With the 2-year falling 2 bp and the 10-year 1 bp, the 2s10s slope on the official constant-maturity basis nudged up 1 bp to +31 bp. The 10-year at 4.40% is its lowest since May 8, 2026 (4.38%), having held at or above 4.41% on every session from May 11 through June 24. The long end kept its small kink, with the 20-year (4.87%) one basis point above the 30-year (4.86%). The modest rally came even as PCE confirmed firm inflation, because the monthly headline landed in line with expectations. [C-2026-06-25-MACRO-C1-TREASURY-CURVE]

The day's headline data was the May PCE price index, released by the Bureau of Economic Analysis at 8:30 a.m. EDT. The Fed's preferred gauge rose 0.4% on the month and 4.1% from a year earlier, while core PCE - excluding food and energy - rose 0.3% on the month and 3.4% year over year, leaving core well above the Fed's 2% target. The BEA's headline year-over-year series has climbed steeply from 2.9% in February to 3.5% in March, 3.8% in April and 4.1% in May. Personal income rose 0.7% and real PCE was unchanged on the month. Crucially for markets, the monthly headline of 0.4% came in line to about a tenth below the 0.5% consensus that had been previewed the day before - which is why Treasuries could rally even on a firm annual inflation reading. [C-2026-06-25-MACRO-C1-PCE]

Crude oil staged the intraday reversal that flipped the macro narrative. In early trade Brent fell to a low of about $72.24 a barrel - its lowest since February 27, before the war, though still above the pre-war level near $66 - with August futures down 1.44% at $72.68 and WTI off 1.08% at $69.58 by 06:39 GMT, weighed by rising regional supply as Oman opened temporary routes and Gulf tankers resumed. Prices then turned higher after a cargo ship was struck by an unknown projectile off the Omani coast, renewing Strait of Hormuz transit-safety concerns, and Brent closed up 1.35% at $74.74 on the Trading Economics CFD. Even with the rebound, Brent is down about 23% over the past month. The bounce re-armed the oil-driven inflation risk that the prior day's collapse had defused. [C-2026-06-25-MACRO-C1-OIL]

The dollar paused its recent run. Trading Economics' ICE DXY basis put the index at 101.45 on June 25, down 0.16% on the day, snapping several sessions of gains; the dollar is still up 2.30% over the past month and 4.43% over twelve months. A softer dollar fit the day's two-sided tape - the in-line monthly PCE eased some of the hawkish pressure, while the renewed Strait of Hormuz risk premium drew bids into oil and gold rather than the dollar. The precise index level carries a vendor and snapshot basis and is held at medium confidence. [C-2026-06-25-MACRO-C1-DOLLAR]

Gold rebounded modestly, rising 0.90% to $4,035.40 an ounce (Trading Economics CFD), though it remains down about 10.5% over the past month and up roughly 21% over the past year. The day's gain lines up with the softer dollar and the return of a Strait of Hormuz safe-haven bid - the same geopolitical impulse that reversed oil. These daily metal moves carry a vendor and spot/CFD basis and are held at medium confidence. [C-2026-06-25-MACRO-C1-GOLD]

The geopolitical backdrop was the day's swing factor. After weeks of de-escalation that had normalized Gulf oil flows - Oman opening temporary routes, a second tanker arriving since the April 24 Strait of Hormuz closure, and Saudi cargoes resuming toward the Ras Tanura terminal - a cargo ship was struck by an unknown projectile off the Omani coast, renewing transit-safety fears in a corridor that carried roughly 20 million barrels a day of oil and products in 2025 (EIA). Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps warned against a new shipping route, and parliament speaker Ghalibaf said Hormuz would never return to its pre-war status and floated transit fees, an idea US Secretary of State Marco Rubio dismissed. This renewed risk premium is what reversed oil higher and revived the gold bid; the precise market-impact attribution is carried at medium confidence. [C-2026-06-25-MACRO-C1-GEOPOLITICS]

Net read: June 25 was a two-sided session that partly reversed the day before. The May PCE print confirmed firm realized inflation - headline 4.1% year over year, core 3.4%, well above target - but the monthly headline landed in line with expectations, so Treasury yields slipped (the 10-year to 4.40%, its lowest since May 8) and the 2s10s nudged to +31 bp. At the same time, the oil-collapse disinflation trade that defined June 24 partly unwound: a cargo-ship strike off Oman reintroduced a Strait of Hormuz risk premium, Brent reversed up 1.35% to $74.74 after an intraday post-war low, gold rose 0.90%, and the dollar eased 0.16%. The macro state now hinges on whether the Hormuz disruption escalates or fades - a sustained one would re-arm the oil-and-inflation channel and pressure a Fed whose core gauge is still running at 3.4%. FX and metals levels carry a vendor basis and the geopolitical impact attribution is held at medium confidence. [C-2026-06-25-MACRO-C1-SYNTHESIS]
