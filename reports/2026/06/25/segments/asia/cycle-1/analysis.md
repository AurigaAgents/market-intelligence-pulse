## Overview
Asia-Pacific equities closed sharply bifurcated on Thursday, June 25, 2026, as a blowout earnings report from Micron Technology reignited a chip and memory-stock rally across the export-driven economies while Hong Kong and Australia lagged. Japan's Nikkei 225 surged 4.61% — its biggest one-day jump in the cycle — and South Korea's KOSPI jumped 5.42%, both led by semiconductor heavyweights. Mainland Chinese benchmarks also rose, with the Shenzhen Component up 1.82% and the ChiNext up 2.84%. Hong Kong's Hang Seng was the regional outlier, falling 1.43% to its lowest close since May 2025, and Australia's ASX 200 slipped 0.66% to a two-week low.

The session's signal is a clean illustration of the Micron earnings cutting across the region in opposite directions: on the demand side, it is a windfall for the Korean and Japanese memory and equipment makers who supply Nvidia and the AI data-center buildout; on the cost side, it is a margin threat for the hardware buyers (Apple, Microsoft) and for consumer-internet ecosystems exposed to the China macro picture. Hong Kong's mega-cap internet complex — Tencent, Meituan, Xiaomi — was hit by the same rotation away from non-chip tech that dragged the US Nasdaq lower, and Australian gold miners and energy names were hit by the softer bullion and oil-back-to-pre-war-levels dynamic.

The macro backdrop was supportive of risk appetite: Brent crude settled up 2% at $75.26 a barrel but remained near pre-US-Iran-war levels as tankers resumed passage through the Strait of Hormuz; the dollar index eased 0.15% to 101.45; and the US 10-year Treasury yield slipped to 4.394%. For oil-import-dependent Japan, the combination of a weaker yen (boosting exporter translations) and soft oil was a double tailwind — even as the yen's proximity to 40-year lows kept intervention risk on the radar.

## Session Status
Thursday, June 25, 2026 was a regular cash-equity and derivatives trading day across all referenced Asian venues (Tokyo Stock Exchange, HKEX, Shanghai/Shenzhen, KRX, ASX), with no market holidays. The session was framed by the after-the-bell Micron Technology fiscal Q3 results from June 24 US trading, which beat sharply and catalyzed a region-wide chip-stock and AI-infrastructure rally. Japan, South Korea, and mainland China benchmarks rose sharply, while Hong Kong and Australia fell.

## Chip and Memory Rally
The single dominant force in the Asia session was a chip and memory-stock rally catalyzed by Micron Technology's after-the-bell fiscal Q3 results from June 24 US trading. Micron reported revenue of $41.46 billion (versus $35.84 billion LSEG consensus), more than quadrupling from $9.3 billion a year earlier, and guided fiscal Q4 revenue to about $50 billion (versus $43.58 billion estimated). CEO Sanjay Mehrotra said tight memory conditions should persist beyond calendar 2027, and the company disclosed $22 billion in customer pre-pay commitments locking in supplies.

In Seoul, the KOSPI's chip heavyweights led: **SK hynix closed +13.91%** and **Samsung Electronics +5.29%** (Trading Economics KOSPI news stream and component table; Reuters corroborated at "13% and 5.3%"). SK hynix was additionally boosted by its announced plan to raise up to KRW 45.5 trillion (~$29.4 billion) through a Nasdaq ADR listing to fund AI chip capacity expansion — a move that J.P. Morgan said could narrow the valuation gap with its smaller US rival Micron. Other Korean gainers included SK Square (+6.61%), SK Inc. (+20.51%), Hyundai Mobis (+1.38%), HD Hyundai Electric (+1.92%), and Kia Corporation (+0.7%).

In Tokyo, the chip-equipment and test-equipment makers led the Nikkei: **Advantest +15.06%**, **Tokyo Electron +7.78%**, **Kioxia Holdings +13.1%**, **Taiyo Yuden +11.3%**, and **Murata Manufacturing +7.9%** (Trading Economics JP225 news stream and component table). Advantest, which tests AI chips for Nvidia, was the single biggest positive force on the Nikkei on a percentage basis.

The rally was not uniform across chip-related names: Arm Holdings and Marvell each fell over 2% in choppy US-listed trading, and Nvidia fell 2.3%, as some investors took profits after the recent run-up. But the Reuters/LSEG chart data cited in the chip-stock wrap showed year-to-date gains as of June 25 of 296% (Micron), 258% (Samsung), 184% (SK Hynix), and 266% (Western Digital) — underscoring that the memory-complex rally has been the dominant equity theme of 2026 in the semiconductor space.

## Hong Kong Divergence
Hong Kong's Hang Seng was the regional outlier, falling 335.27 points (-1.43%) to 23,076.91 — its lowest close since May 2025 — even as Korea and Japan surged on the same Micron catalyst. The slide came as investors remained cautious ahead of Hong Kong's trade balance data and as the mega-cap internet and consumer names that dominate the index dragged: **Tencent -1.73%, Meituan -2.44%, Xiaomi -2.87%, Sunny Optical Technology -11.7%, and Trip.com -10.9%** (Trading Economics HK50 component table and news stream). The divergence between the chip-driven Korea/Japan rallies and the Hong Kong underperformance was the session's defining cross-market structure, and it mirrors the US pattern in which the Magnificent 7 retreat outweighed the Micron-led memory rally on the cap-weighted indexes.

Strong southbound inflows from mainland China — which have been supporting Hong Kong equities amid a revival in IPO activity — partly tempered losses, but were not enough to overcome the risk-off tone in the internet and consumer sectors. The HSBC close (+0.07%) was essentially flat.

## Mainland China
Mainland Chinese benchmarks closed higher, with breadth favoring growth boards. The Shanghai Composite added 0.23% to 4,120.28 and the Shenzhen Component jumped 1.82% to 16,344.08, per Xinhua's official close summary. The ChiNext Index gained 2.84% to 4,371.99 and the STAR Composite rose 2.1% to 2,391.95. The CSI 300 closed at 5,020.10, up 1.56%.

Combined turnover on the Shanghai and Shenzhen indices reached 3.59 trillion yuan (~$526.32 billion), up from 3.28 trillion yuan the prior session (Xinhua). Shares related to memory chips and ultracapacitors led the gains, while the precious-metals sector saw major declines — the same memory-vs-gold pattern seen across the region.

## Australia
Australia's ASX 200 slipped 58.50 points (-0.66%) to 8,749.90, a two-week low, erasing modest gains from the previous session. The macro driver was domestic: a stronger-than-expected rise in Australia's May employment added weight to expectations that the Reserve Bank of Australia will keep its hawkish stance, with Deputy Governor Andrew Hauser stressing that inflation remains "too high" and that more work may be needed (Trading Economics ASX news stream).

The sector pattern was the mirror image of the chip-led rallies to the north. **Gold miners slid as bullion hit a seven-month low**: Evolution Mining -3.9%, Northern Star Resources -3.1%. **Energy names fell as oil retreated to pre-Iran-war levels**: Woodside -3.3%, Santos -2.6%. **Judo Capital plunged 39%** after cutting its 2026 earnings outlook. The **big four banks shed between 1.2% and 3.7%**: CBA -1.27%, Westpac -2.01%, NAB -3.35%, ANZ -2.19%. Losses were partly capped by firmer US stock futures overnight.

## Yen and FX Backdrop
The yen traded at 161.80 per dollar, flat on the day but near its lowest level in 40 years, with more intervention widely expected from Tokyo after the last bout around May did not stem the currency's decline (Reuters global markets wrapup). The Bank of Japan had raised its policy rate to 1.00% (from 0.75%), and BoJ board member Naoki Tamura — identified by Trading Economics as a hawk — called for rate hikes every few months. Japan's headline CPI was 1.50% year-over-year in May, up from 1.40% in April.

The weak-yen backdrop supports the translation value of Japanese exporters' overseas earnings and was a tailwind for the Nikkei's chip-led rally, even as it raises intervention risk. The dollar index fell 0.15% to 101.45 and the euro was at $1.137, a whisker above Wednesday's 13-month low.

## Cross-Asset and Macro Context
The cross-asset backdrop was broadly supportive of risk appetite. Brent crude settled up 2% at $75.26 a barrel but remained near pre-US-Iran-war levels as tankers resumed passage through the Strait of Hormuz and peace-talk progress was signaled (Reuters). The US 10-year Treasury yield slipped 0.59 bp to 4.394%, easing pressure on long-duration equity valuations. Spot gold rose 0.64% to $4,026.09 an ounce in NY trading, though Asian-session gold miners sold off on the weaker bullion tone that prevailed during their cash session.

For oil-import-dependent Japan, the combination of a weaker yen (boosting exporter translations) and soft oil (easing import-cost pressure) was a double tailwind. For Australia, the weaker oil and gold hit the energy and materials sectors that dominate the ASX. For Hong Kong, the cautious pre-data-risk tone overwhelmed the supportive macro backdrop.

On policy, Chicago Fed President Austan Goolsbee called US core inflation "too high" and "trending the wrong way," consistent with a majority of Fed officials favoring a rate hike later this year, after the in-line May PCE release. The combination — three-year-high US headline PCE, hawkish Fed commentary, and a renewed-but-fading Middle East oil-supply risk — keeps the global rates-and-inflation question firmly at the center of the macro narrative for the next Asia session.
