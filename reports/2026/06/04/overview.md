# Global Markets — June 4, 2026

## So what

Markets moved from record-chase to guarded rotation. The US rally paused after a hot ADP payroll print pushed the 10-year back toward the 4.5% valuation line and hit tech futures hardest. Asia followed Wall Street lower, Europe traded risk-off on a mix of energy/geopolitics and chip weakness, and sentiment cooled from greed toward neutral. The twist is macro: Brent fell below $97 as ceasefire and Iran-talk hopes unwound part of the oil premium. That means today is not panic. It is a confidence test before payrolls, with rates, tech leadership, and oil headlines all pulling on the same risk budget.

## Cross-segment synthesis

The US signal is narrower than the headline red tape suggests. The S&P 500 closed June 3 at 7,553.68 after snapping an eight-day win streak, the Nasdaq fell 0.89%, and Dow lost 1.21%. Pre-market futures then showed a sharper growth hit: NDX futures were down about 1.26%, while Dow futures tried to stabilize. That spread is the tell. Markets are not abandoning equities; they are repricing the cost of owning duration after ADP reported 122,000 May private-sector jobs versus a much softer consensus. The 10-year near 4.5% is again the ceiling for mega-cap multiples.

Asia confirmed the same profit-taking impulse. Nikkei closed at 67,470, down 1.36%; Hang Seng fell around 1.4%; KOSPI lost roughly 1.8%; Shanghai was weaker but more resilient. The region is reacting to the stalled US rally and to local policy pressure around USD/JPY just below 160. The reviewer correctly flagged the Iran story as secondary and somewhat conflicted across sources. For the daily synthesis, the clean read is simpler: Asia is following the US valuation reset, with yen intervention risk as the local amplifier.

Europe is where the tape looks most fragile. DAX and CAC were down, FTSE was modestly lower, and Euro STOXX 50 remained in a risk-off snapshot despite live quote noise. The corrected analyst cycle matters: Europe is not higher today. The region is absorbing two shocks at once. First, imported-energy risk remains equity-negative even when crude is falling on a particular day, because Hormuz and Iran headlines can reprice inflation expectations quickly. Second, Broadcom's weaker revenue print hit the global chip complex and therefore Europe's ASML/Infineon/STMicroelectronics/SAP channel.

Macro complicates the bearish story. Brent at $96.58 was down 1.26% and has drawn down more than 12% on the month, while gold slipped only 0.22% and copper stayed firm. That is not a simple growth scare. It is a market unwinding some war premium while still keeping hedges for a binary geopolitical path. If talks and ceasefire implementation become credible, oil relief can support margins and risk assets. If the diplomatic path breaks, the $100 Brent line returns immediately.

Sentiment says the market still has a cushion, but not a free one. CNN Fear & Greed is neutral at 54, unchanged from the prior close but down from 60 one week ago and 66 one month ago. VIX rose to 16.62 from 16.06, yet remains far below crisis levels. AAII is almost balanced, with bulls at 36.3% and bears at 37.0%. This is a stalling-neutral regime: investors are less eager to chase, but they are not yet forced sellers.

Base case: choppy consolidation into payrolls. Upside requires the 10-year to reject 4.5%, Brent to stay below $100, and tech weakness to remain contained. The downgrade line is equally clear: hot payrolls, Nasdaq losing 30,200, or VIX above 18.5 would turn a rotation day into a defensive one.
