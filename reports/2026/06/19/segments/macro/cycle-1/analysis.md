## Analysis

June 19 was a holiday-thinned macro session with the U.S. dark. SIFMA recommended a full close of the U.S. dollar-denominated fixed income market for Juneteenth, and U.S. equity cash markets were shut, so there was no Wall Street session and no June 19 Treasury par-yield print. The day's signal therefore came from global FX, ICE Brent and European cash rates rather than from any U.S. tape. [C-2026-06-19-MACRO-C1-US-HOLIDAY]

That left U.S. rates frozen at Thursday's close. The official Treasury par-yield CSV has no June 19 row; its last print stays June 18 at 2-year 4.19%, 10-year 4.46% and 30-year 4.90%, a +27 bp 2s10s spread that had flattened from +29 bp on June 17. Inference: the post-FOMC repricing from earlier in the week was still the standing U.S. rates picture going into the long weekend, neither extended nor unwound by the holiday. [C-2026-06-19-MACRO-C1-TREASURY-CARRY]

The dollar did the talking instead. The U.S. Dollar Index finished little changed near 100.80 (about -0.05%) but printed a fresh 13-month, 52-week intraday high of 101.13 before easing, holding just above the June 18 close of 100.849. Reuters framed it as the dollar's strongest weekly gain in about a month, achieved mostly at the yen's expense - the yen traded near a 40-year low, its weakest since late 1986, having fallen in five of the last six weeks and drawing intervention warnings from Tokyo. Inference: with the U.S. closed, the dollar's strength was a relative-rate and safe-haven story running through the FX channel, not a fresh U.S. catalyst. [C-2026-06-19-MACRO-C1-DXY, C-2026-06-19-MACRO-C1-SESSION-DRIVER]

European rates leaned the same way. Germany's 10-year Bund yield rose to roughly 2.96%-2.99% on June 19 - Trading Economics dated it 2.96% (+4 bp) and the WSJ 2.992% (+6 bp), a disclosed ~3 bp vendor spread, with the Bundesbank daily-yield table for 19.06.2026 the official primary reference. The UK 10-year gilt held around 4.84% at the London close (FT 4.84, +0.003 pp; WSJ 4.846%, +0.078 pp), broadly steady at an elevated level. With Bunds and gilts both edging up while the U.S. curve sat static, the modest tightening impulse on the day was a European-hours phenomenon. [C-2026-06-19-MACRO-C1-BUND, C-2026-06-19-MACRO-C1-GILT]

The one hard data print reinforced the energy-inflation theme. Destatis reported that German producer prices rose 2.2% year on year and 0.3% on the month in May 2026 - the fastest annual pace since May 2023, yet below the 2.5% y/y and 0.7% m/m forecasts. The mix is the story: intermediate goods up 4.2% and energy up 2.5% year on year, with mineral-oil products up 34.9% on the Iran and Middle East conflict (naphtha +60.9%, heating oil +52.8%, motor fuels +24.2%), even as non-durable consumer goods fell 1.7% and ex-energy PPI rose a steadier 2.3%. Inference: pipeline price pressure is still real and oil-driven, but the below-consensus headline and softer monthly pace argue the supply-shock impulse is plateauing rather than re-accelerating. [C-2026-06-19-MACRO-C1-GER-PPI]

Oil itself confirmed the plateau. Brent rebounded to about $80.59/bbl, up 0.93% (range $78.80-$80.82; Trading Economics cross-checks $80.58), after dipping below $80 intraday and erasing earlier losses once U.S. Vice President JD Vance pulled out of Friday's Iran talks. The bounce capped a sharp week-long selloff driven by the U.S.-Iran deal reopening the Strait of Hormuz, where tankers resumed transit after the U.S. lifted its blockade on Thursday; RBC Capital Markets cautioned that the reopening trajectory may stay well below pre-crisis throughput even if the deal holds. U.S. WTI traded only on CME's shortened Juneteenth session, so Brent carried the benchmark read. [C-2026-06-19-MACRO-C1-OIL-BRENT, C-2026-06-19-MACRO-C1-SESSION-DRIVER]

Net read: with the U.S. absent for Juneteenth, June 19 was a low-volume continuation, not a new theme. The dollar near 13-month highs, Bund and gilt yields nudging up, a still-firm but below-forecast German PPI, and a Brent bounce off sub-$80 all point to the same backdrop - a market still leaning toward policy restraint and a fragile, only-partly-priced Middle East de-escalation - rather than a decisive shift in either direction. The pulled Iran talks and yen near 40-year lows are the two live risks to watch when full U.S. trading resumes.
