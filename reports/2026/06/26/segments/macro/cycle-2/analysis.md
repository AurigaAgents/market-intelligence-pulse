## Analysis

The macro tape remained inflation-led. BEA's May release showed the PCE price index rising 0.4% month over month and 4.1% year over year, while core PCE rose 0.3% month over month and 3.4% year over year. CNBC's policy story framed the headline annual rate as the highest since April 2023 and core as the highest since October 2023. That keeps the inflation claim anchored in the official release while using CNBC only for market-policy context. [C-2026-06-26-MACRO-C2-PCE-INFLATION]

Demand did not crack in the same release. BEA reported personal income up $181.6 billion / 0.7%, disposable personal income up $164.9 billion / 0.7%, current-dollar PCE up $156.1 billion / 0.7%, and real PCE up $43.8 billion / 0.3%. Personal outlays rose $159.9 billion, personal saving was $704.2 billion, and the saving rate was 3.0%. The clean read is sticky inflation plus still-positive real spending, not a simple growth scare. [C-2026-06-26-MACRO-C2-INCOME-SPENDING]

Policy risk hardened rather than faded. CNBC reported that Minneapolis Fed President Neel Kashkari changed his year-end view from one rate cut to one rate hike, while saying the move was still conditional on incoming data. He cited supply dynamics from tariffs, fertilizer disruption, energy and oil prices around the Strait of Hormuz, and massive data-center investment pressure. That links the inflation problem to more than crude alone and makes the AI-capex cycle part of the macro supply story. [C-2026-06-26-MACRO-C2-FED-POLICY-RISK]

Treasury yields still eased on the day. The official Treasury par curve showed the 2-year at 4.07%, 5-year at 4.12%, 10-year at 4.38%, 20-year at 4.87%, and 30-year at 4.87% on 06/26/2026. Against 06/25/2026, that is -2 bp on the 2-year, -3 bp on the 5-year, -2 bp on the 10-year, flat on the 20-year, and +1 bp on the 30-year; 2s10s stayed at +31 bp. Reuters separately reported the benchmark 10-year down 1.16 bp to 4.38% and the 2-year down 2.48 bp to 4.096%, consistent with a lower-yield Friday session despite sticky inflation. [C-2026-06-26-MACRO-C2-TREASURY-CURVE]

The cross-asset relief channel was oil, but the rest of the tape stayed defensive. Reuters reported Brent crude futures down 4.34% to settle at $72/bbl as more tankers left the Strait of Hormuz and Saudi Aramco resumed loading at Ras Tanura. The same page showed the dollar index down 0.16% to 101.35 but headed for a second weekly gain, USD/JPY near 161.76 and close to 40-year yen lows, and spot gold up 1.06% to $4,068.72/oz. Oil relief lowered immediate inflation pressure, but weak-yen and higher-gold signals kept policy and geopolitical stress visible. [C-2026-06-26-MACRO-C2-OIL-FX-GOLD]

The final June Michigan survey also improved without normalizing. Consumer sentiment rose to 49.5 from 44.8 in May, a 10.5% monthly gain, but remained 18.5% below June 2025. Year-ahead inflation expectations edged down to 4.6% from 4.8%; long-run expectations fell to 3.3% from 3.9%. Director Joanne Hsu said gas-price moderation helped, but the cost of living remained prominent and more than half of consumers spontaneously mentioned high prices for the third straight month. [C-2026-06-26-MACRO-C2-CONSUMER-SENTIMENT]

Global risk tone did not validate a clean risk-on story. Reuters reported MSCI's world-stock gauge down 0.53% and set for a 2% weekly loss. European shares fell nearly 0.7%, European technology lost 1.17%, MSCI Asia ex-Japan fell nearly 3%, and chip stocks lost 5.3% on the day and were set for a 7.7% weekly loss. The S&P 500 and Nasdaq also notched weekly losses. The cycle-2 conclusion is therefore mixed-to-defensive: oil and yields relieved part of the inflation shock, but sticky PCE, a hawkish Fed signal, weak yen pressure, elevated gold, and tech-led global equity weakness kept the macro backdrop fragile. [C-2026-06-26-MACRO-C2-GLOBAL-RISK-TONE]
