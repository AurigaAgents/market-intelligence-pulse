## Overview
Asia-Pacific equities plunged on Friday, June 26, 2026, as traders aggressively locked in profits from the AI-driven rallies that had pushed both the Nikkei 225 and KOSPI to record highs earlier in the week. The session was the mirror image of Thursday's Micron-led surge: Japan's Nikkei 225 dropped 3,271 points (-4.52%) to 69,095 and South Korea's KOSPI plunged 519.09 points (-5.81%) to 8,411.21, undoing most of the prior session's gains. Mainland China's Shanghai Composite fell 2.26% to 4,027.26 and the Shenzhen Component dropped 3.44% to 15,782.22. Hong Kong's Hang Seng extended its decline, losing 405.05 points (-1.76%) to 22,671.86. Australia's ASX 200 was the sole regional gainer, edging up 14.30 points (+0.16%) to 8,764.20 as rising gold and copper prices supported miners.

MSCI's index of Asian stocks outside Japan fell nearly 3% (Reuters). The catalyst was multi-layered: profit-taking after the record-high runs, Apple's price hikes on iPads and Macs fueling structural-inflation worries from soaring memory costs, the Nasdaq's extended losing streak, and US core PCE inflation reaching 3.4% — the highest since October 2023 — reinforcing expectations the Fed could keep rates higher for longer. AP described the session plainly: "Shares plunged Friday in Asia, led by heavy losses in Japan and South Korea as traders sold to lock in gains from recent rallies in stocks related to artificial intelligence."

The sharp reversal underscores the two-way volatility that has defined the AI trade. As AP noted: "The movements were typical of recent volatility as investors react to developments in the AI sector." Both the Nikkei and KOSPI hit all-time highs earlier in the week — the Nikkei at 73,007 and the KOSPI at 9,385.59. The Friday plunge wiped out much of the preceding rally, demonstrating that the market is in a choppy, rotational phase rather than a clean trend.

## Session Status
Friday, June 26, 2026 was a regular cash-equity and derivatives trading day across all referenced Asian venues (Tokyo Stock Exchange, HKEX, Shanghai/Shenzhen, KRX, ASX), with no market holidays. The session context is critical: it followed Thursday's blockbuster Micron-led rally (Nikkei +4.61%, KOSPI +5.42%), which itself followed a Tuesday tech selloff and Wednesday stabilization. The Friday session reversed the Thursday rally violently, as profit-taking, Apple's price hikes, and the Nasdaq's extended losing streak drove heavy selling. Both the Nikkei and KOSPI had hit all-time highs earlier in the week (73,007 and 9,385.59, respectively).

## Chip and Tech Reversal
The single dominant force in the Asia session was a violent reversal of Thursday's chip and memory-stock rally. In Tokyo, the losses were led by **SoftBank Group (-12.19%)**, **Taiyo Yuden (-11.48%)**, **Advantest (-10.67%)**, **Tokyo Electron (-4.26%)**, and **Kioxia Holdings (-4.1%)** (Trading Economics JP225 news stream and component table). In Seoul, **Samsung Electronics fell -6.28%, SK Hynix -8.95%, SK Square -9.43%, Hyundai Motor -4.67%, LG Energy Solution -6.25%, Hanwha Aerospace -6.27%, Doosan Enerbility -8.20%, and HD Hyundai Heavy Industries -3.09%** (Trading Economics KOSPI news stream). In Hong Kong, **SMIC dropped -6.8%** (Trading Economics HK50 news stream).

The catalyst was multi-layered. First, profit-taking was inevitable after both the Nikkei and KOSPI hit record highs earlier in the week (73,007 and 9,385.59, respectively). Second, Apple's price hikes on iPads and Macs — announced June 25 and attributed to soaring memory chip and input storage costs — fueled worries about structural inflation from massive AI spending. Third, the Nasdaq extended its losing streak overnight, and US core PCE inflation reached 3.4% (highest since October 2023), reinforcing expectations that the Fed could keep rates higher for longer.

The reversal underscores the two-way volatility that has defined the AI trade. AP noted: "The movements were typical of recent volatility as investors react to developments in the AI sector." The KOSPI's -5.81% and the Nikkei's -4.52% were among the worst daily performances in the current cycle. The XTB Morning Wrap described the KOSPI's move as a "violent tech unwinding" after a "monster 70% rally in the second quarter."

## Hong Kong
Hong Kong's Hang Seng fell 405.05 points (-1.76%) to 22,671.86, extending its decline from the prior session. The slide was broad-based: technology stocks led the decline, while financial and producer manufacturing shares also came under pressure. Notable laggards: **Tencent -2.28%, AIA -1.94%, SMIC -6.8%, Meituan -2.80%, Xiaomi -3.95%, and Kingboard Laminates -5.2%** (Trading Economics HK50 component table and news stream). Investors remained cautious despite the Hong Kong debut of Apple supplier Lingyi iTech, whose HK$8.3 billion IPO underscored continued interest in AI-related listings but failed to improve broader market sentiment. The Hang Seng's losses on Thursday and Friday combined have deepened the pullback from the spring highs.

## Mainland China
Mainland Chinese benchmarks closed sharply lower. The Shanghai Composite fell 2.26% to 4,027.26 and the Shenzhen Component dropped 3.44% to 15,782.22, per Xinhua's official close summary. The ChiNext Index lost 4.07% to 4,194.21 and the STAR Composite fell 2.02% to 2,343.66. The CSI 300 declined 3.03% to 4,868.22 (Trading Economics).

Combined turnover on the Shanghai and Shenzhen indices reached 3.55 trillion yuan (~$521 billion), down from 3.59 trillion yuan the prior session (Xinhua). Shares in the education and pork sectors led gains, while cobalt metal and battery industry stocks posted sharp declines — a rotation away from the battery/EV supply chain that mirrored the broader regional tech/chip selloff.

## Australia
Australia's ASX 200 was the sole regional gainer, edging up 14.30 points (+0.16%) to 8,764.20. The advance was driven by process industries, retail trade, and transport stocks, and by rising copper and gold prices that supported miners: **BHP +0.8%, Rio Tinto +2.2%, and gold producers Northern Star and Evolution Mining each up about 3%** as easing fears of an imminent US rate hike lifted bullion. Three of the big four banks rose modestly: Westpac +0.23%, NAB +0.16%, ANZ +0.52%, while CBA slipped -0.42%. CSL fell -2.36%.

However, the local market fell 0.7% for the week, reversing strength in the prior two sessions. Resilience in recent data stoked concern that the RBA may tighten further to rein in inflation, after pausing following three hikes since January. Traders also turned cautious ahead of China's PMI data next week and the RBA's June meeting minutes.

## Yen and FX Backdrop
The yen teetered near its weakest level against the dollar in 40 years at 161.62, beyond the 160 level that many see as a "line in the sand" for Japanese authorities (Reuters). The dollar index eased 0.3% to 101.20 but was headed for a second straight weekly gain. The euro rose 0.33% to $1.1407 but was set for a second consecutive weekly loss.

On the macro front, Tokyo's core CPI accelerated to 1.6% y/y in June from 1.3% in May — the first acceleration in eight months — matching consensus forecasts. The core-core index (excluding fresh food and energy) quickened to 1.9% from 1.6%, suggesting that Middle East energy shocks and May's 6.3% wholesale inflation spike are filtering into non-energy items like food. This reinforces expectations that the BoJ will continue raising interest rates. BoJ board member Naoki Tamura, identified by Trading Economics as a hawk, called for rate hikes every few months. The Bank of Japan's policy rate stands at 1.00%, up from 0.75%.

## Cross-Asset and Macro Context
The cross-asset backdrop was mixed. Brent crude futures plunged 4.24% to $72.07 per barrel as more tankers left the Strait of Hormuz and Saudi Aramco resumed loading at its Ras Tanura terminal after a nearly four-month halt (Reuters, LSEG shipping data). However, a Singapore-flagged cargo vessel (Ever Lovely) was hit by a projectile 7.5 nautical miles off Oman's port of Dahit, prompting the IMO to pause its operation to evacuate over 11,000 stranded seafarers from the Strait (XTB Morning Wrap). Spot gold rose 1.5% to $4,086.29 an ounce as easing fears of an imminent US rate hike lifted bullion. The US 10-year Treasury yield fell 1.75 bp to 4.375%.

For oil-import-dependent Asia (Japan, Korea), falling oil was a tailwind, but it was overwhelmed by the equity selloff. For Australia, rising gold and copper supported the ASX's outperformance. On US policy, Minneapolis Fed President Neel Kashkari said he had changed his outlook from one rate cut by year-end to one rate hike by year-end (CNBC, per US cycle), and May headline PCE inflation rose to 4.1% — the highest since April 2023. The combination of hawkish Fed commentary, hot PCE, and fading Middle East oil-supply risk keeps the global rates-and-inflation question at the center of the macro narrative.
