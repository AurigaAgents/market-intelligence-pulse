## Overview
US equities ended Friday, June 26, 2026, with a narrow cap-weighted decline but better internals than the index tape suggested. The S&P 500 slipped 3.47 points, or less than 0.1%, to 7,354.02; the Dow Jones Industrial Average fell 44.51 points, or 0.1%, to 51,876.11; the Nasdaq Composite lost 60.99 points, or 0.2%, to 25,297.62; and the Russell 2000 added 2.23 points, or 0.1%, to 3,010.08. For the week, the S&P 500 lost 2.0% and the Nasdaq lost 4.6%, while the Dow gained 0.6% and the Russell 2000 gained 1.0%.

The main signal was rotation, not broad liquidation. AP reported that nearly two out of every three S&P 500 stocks rose, but AI-linked losses still kept the cap-weighted market down. Micron Technology fell 6.7% and was the heaviest weight on the market; ON Semiconductor dropped 23.7% after agreeing to buy Synaptics in an all-stock deal valued near $7 billion. The AI pressure was global: SoftBank fell 12.5% in Japan, SK Hynix lost 8.4%, Samsung Electronics sank 5.3%, and South Korea's Kospi fell 5.8%.

At the same time, easing oil prices and lower yields cushioned the broader market. Brent crude fell 3.8% to $72.60, below its level before the US and Israel attacked Iran, and the 10-year Treasury yield eased to 4.37% from 4.40% late Thursday. Health care, consumer staples, utilities, and other defensive or fuel-sensitive groups drew bids. The Fed backdrop stayed hawkish: Minneapolis Fed President Neel Kashkari said he now expects one rate hike this year, while CNBC reported May headline PCE inflation at 4.1% and core PCE at 3.4%.

## Session Status
Friday was a regular US market session for the cash-equity basis used in this segment. AP's close summary provides final Friday levels for all four major indexes and weekly/year-to-date performance, so the report uses the US regular-session close as the primary market basis. The session closed a volatile week in which the Nasdaq's 4.6% decline contrasted with gains in the Dow and Russell 2000.

## Index Closes
The final index tape was mixed but narrowly negative for the largest cap-weighted benchmarks:

- **S&P 500**: 7,354.02, down 3.47 points, or less than 0.1%. Week: -2.0%. Year to date: +7.4%.
- **Dow Jones Industrial Average**: 51,876.11, down 44.51 points, or 0.1%. Week: +0.6%. Year to date: +7.9%.
- **Nasdaq Composite**: 25,297.62, down 60.99 points, or 0.2%. Week: -4.6%. Year to date: +8.8%.
- **Russell 2000**: 3,010.08, up 2.23 points, or 0.1%. Week: +1.0%. Year to date: +21.3%.

The S&P 500 and Nasdaq both recorded weekly losses, but the Dow and Russell 2000 finished the week higher. That split matters: it shows the drawdown remained concentrated in technology and AI-linked market leadership rather than spreading uniformly across the US equity complex.

## Breadth and Rotation
The session's internal structure was stronger than the headline S&P 500 move. AP reported that nearly two out of every three stocks within the S&P 500 rose, yet the index still slipped. This is the same cap-weight problem seen earlier in the week: a small number of large AI and technology names can overwhelm broad gains because of their index weight.

Defensive and fuel-sensitive groups helped stabilize the tape. AP reported Eli Lilly rose 7.1% after a European Medicines Agency committee recommendation, and American Airlines gained 1.7% as lower oil prices helped companies with large fuel costs. CNBC added that health care was heading for a weekly gain of more than 7%, while consumer staples, financials, and utilities rose on Friday. In contrast, CNBC reported the S&P 500 information technology sector fell 1%.

## AI and Semiconductor Pressure
AI-linked equities remained the market's pressure point. AP identified Micron Technology's 6.7% decline as the heaviest weight on the market. The stock had been a major 2026 winner because AI demand boosted memory pricing, but the same price surge became a concern after Apple raised prices on laptops and other products to offset higher memory costs. The worry is that higher end-product prices could eventually slow demand.

ON Semiconductor was the day's largest S&P 500 loser, dropping 23.7% after agreeing to buy Synaptics in an all-stock deal valued at roughly $7 billion. SpaceX showed the volatility around the AI-linked IPO complex: AP reported it briefly fell 2.9% below $149, then swung to a 3.5% gain before finishing up 0.2%. The global spillover was sharper. SoftBank fell 12.5% in Japan after a New York Times report that OpenAI may delay its IPO to next year; SK Hynix lost 8.4%, Samsung Electronics fell 5.3%, and South Korea's Kospi dropped 5.8%.

## Oil, Rates, and Inflation
Falling oil prices were the main macro relief valve. AP reported Brent crude down 3.8% to $72.60, below where it stood before the US and Israel attacked Iran. Lower oil helped Treasury yields ease: the 10-year yield fell to 4.37% from 4.40% late Thursday. AP also cited a decline in one-year US consumer inflation expectations to 4.6% from 4.8% in May.

That relief did not remove Fed risk. CNBC reported that Minneapolis Fed President Neel Kashkari changed his outlook from one rate cut by year-end to one rate hike by year-end, citing Middle East supply risk, tariffs, energy and fertilizer disruptions, and massive data-center investment. CNBC also reported May headline PCE inflation at 4.1%, the highest since April 2023, and core PCE at 3.4%, with inflation above the Fed's 2% goal for five years.
