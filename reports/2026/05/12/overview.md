# Markets Overview — 2026-05-12

## So What

Tuesday's tape changed the question from “can risk assets absorb expensive oil?” to “can they absorb expensive oil plus a fresh inflation shock?” The approved bundle points to a broadly risk-off session: hot April CPI pushed US yields and the dollar higher, Brent stayed bid near $107.58 as the Iran/Hormuz stress remained unresolved, Europe had to digest oil, UK political risk, and hawkish ECB repricing at once, and Asia saw profit taking in the most crowded AI-beta pocket. The exception is sentiment: investors are still call-heavy and the Fear & Greed index remains in Greed, so this is not capitulation. It is a market whose positioning still looks optimistic while the macro math has become materially less friendly.

## Risk Dashboard

1. **The CPI print moved the anchor.** The US 2Y at 4.95% and 10Y above 4.60% say the market is not treating April inflation as a rounding error. A core +0.4% MoM print, in an oil-shock environment, makes the Fed-cut timeline harder to defend.

2. **Oil is still the global transmission belt.** Brent at $107.58 is not just an energy-sector story. It feeds European import costs, keeps inflation expectations tense, and reinforces the US rates reaction by raising future pass-through risk.

3. **Europe has the worst policy mix.** German CPI at +2.9%, Bund yields above 3%, and UK gilt stress around 5% leave European equities exposed to both margin pressure and duration pressure. The index decline is orderly, but the factor mix is poor.

4. **Asia dispersion matters.** The KOSPI drop says the AI-memory trade can de-gross quickly when crowded. Japan's gain prevents a region-wide panic call, but Korea's reversal is a warning against assuming last week's leadership is still clean.

5. **Sentiment is supportive, but vulnerable.** Fear & Greed near 67 and a 0.53 equity put/call ratio can cushion pullbacks if macro news improves. They also mean the market is not priced for a prolonged rates-and-oil squeeze.

6. **Gold is not giving a clean haven signal.** Spot gold remains historically high, but the day's decline shows that when real yields and the dollar rise together, safe-haven demand is not enough by itself.

## Cross-Segment Synthesis

The day is a double-entry ledger. One side still records risk appetite: ETF inflows are positive, put/call ratios are low, Fear & Greed is in Greed, and Japan managed to close higher. The other side records a much harsher macro balance: hotter CPI, higher Treasury yields, stronger dollar, Brent above $107, European policy stress, and Korean AI profit taking.

That split explains why the session feels risk-off without looking like forced liquidation. Investors have not abandoned the equity trend, but the discount rate has moved against them. The US CPI print is the cleanest new information because it turns the oil shock from a geopolitical problem into a central-bank problem. If inflation is already hot before the full energy pass-through arrives, the market has to reprice not only earnings multiples but also the probability that policy easing is unavailable when growth slows.

The most important cross-asset confirmation is the combination of Nasdaq underperformance, a stronger dollar, higher front-end yields, and lower gold. That is not a generic fear trade. It is a real-rate trade. Long-duration assets are being marked down because the policy path has hardened, while commodities remain supported by physical supply stress. Europe's weakness fits the same template, and Asia's split shows that even strong local stories can be overwhelmed when positioning is crowded.

The relief path is clear but narrow: Hormuz needs a credible normalization signal, or the next inflation data must break softer enough to offset the energy premium. Without that, Tuesday's optimistic sentiment readings become less a cushion than a vulnerability.
