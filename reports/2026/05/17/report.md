## Overview

## Opening Take

The "so what" from Friday's close is that this is no longer a clean equity-momentum market. The common chain across regions is oil risk turning into inflation risk, inflation risk turning into bond-yield risk, and bond-yield risk forcing investors to reprice the highest-duration parts of the equity book. US stocks pulled back from records, Europe sold off on Bund and gilt stress, Asia lost momentum after a light Trump-Xi deliverable, and sentiment stayed constructive only because investors hedged rather than liquidated. The market is not broken, but the next bullish confirmation has to come from rates and crude, not from another index headline.

Cash markets were closed over the weekend, so this report anchors to the verified Friday, May 15 settlement tape.

## What Drove the Day

**1. Bonds became the market's referee.** The US analyst and macro retry converge on the same corrected number: the US 10-year closed around 4.59%, not the stale 4.41% level rejected in cycle one. That matters because the market has already crossed the 4.50% psychological pin. The next stress marker is 4.65%; above that, the equity market has to discount not only a higher Fed path but a wider term-premium regime. The Russell 2000's 2.44% decline is the cleanest equity expression of that stress.

**2. Europe confirmed this was global, not just US.** The STOXX 600 fell 1.48%, the DAX lost 2.07%, and the CAC slipped back below 8,000. The European tape was hit by the same energy-inflation channel, but with local amplifiers: Bund yields near 3.15%, ECB June hike expectations, and UK political risk that pushed gilts toward 5.185% while sterling weakened for a fifth straight day. Mining weakness, especially the Antofagasta/Fresnillo pressure flagged by the reviewer as directionally right, shows that real-rate pressure mattered more than classic safe-haven behavior.

**3. Asia lost the momentum handoff.** The KOSPI's reversal from above 8,000 to 7,493.18 was the most violent single index signal in the set, with the reviewer confirming the roughly 6.1% drop. Japan added a separate rates channel: BoJ hawkish pressure and USD/JPY near 158.77 kept the Nikkei under stress. China was the exception, with Shanghai slightly higher after the Trump-Xi summit avoided new friction. But "no new friction" is not the same as a fresh growth impulse.

**4. Sentiment is hedged greed, not panic.** CNN Fear & Greed at 63 says investors are still in risk-taking mode. VIX at 18.43, put/call cooling from complacency, and positive ETF issuance say the same thing in a more nuanced way: capital has not left the market, but it is paying for insurance. That is healthier than blind euphoria, but it also means the tape is vulnerable if crude and yields gap higher together.

## Cross-Segment Synthesis

The best synthesis is **Neutral-to-Risk-Off with a stagflationary bias**. The bearish case is not that equities fell on Friday; it is that the same upstream shock explains almost every weak point: crude lifted inflation fears, inflation lifted sovereign yields, yields lifted the dollar and real-rate pressure, and real-rate pressure hit equities, gold, miners, and small caps. The copper decline is the useful dissent. It says this is not a clean global-demand reflation. It is closer to a cost-push shock where energy rises while industrial demand signals wobble.

That distinction matters for next week. If oil stabilizes and the 10-year retreats below the 4.55%-4.60% zone, the market can frame Friday as an insurance-buying pause inside an intact bull trend. If Brent pushes through $110-$112 and the US 10-year tests 4.65%, the same positioning becomes a vulnerability: long exposure remains high enough that hedged greed can turn into active de-risking. The bull case survives, but it is now conditional on lower volatility in the two assets that matter most: crude and duration.

## Deep Dive

## The Hook

Friday's defining story was not that stocks fell. It was that the market faced the wrong kind of inflation shock at the wrong point in the policy cycle. Crude oil rose on renewed Iran and Strait of Hormuz risk, April CPI had already re-accelerated to 3.8% year over year, and the US 10-year yield closed around 4.59%. That combination changed the discount rate under almost every asset class. Equities, Europe, Asia, gold, copper, and sentiment all reacted to the same question: what if higher energy prices arrive while central banks have little room to ease?

## The Chain Reaction

The macro analyst's corrected cycle-two report is the center of the day. WTI closed around $105.57, Brent near $109.17, and crude was up more than 7% on the week. The trigger was not a clean demand boom. It was a supply-risk premium: the US rejection of Iran's latest proposal and renewed concern around Strait of Hormuz disruption. In a normal reflation, copper should confirm the story. It did not. Copper fell 4.81% Friday, which makes the setup more uncomfortable: energy inflation is rising while industrial demand is at least questioning the growth impulse.

That is why the bond market mattered so much. The first macro cycle was rejected for a stale US 10-year figure; the accepted retry put the close at 4.59%, up roughly 11 basis points and already through the 4.50% psychological pin. That one correction changes the severity of the whole tape. A 4.59% 10-year does not merely pressure mortgages or bank funding. It compresses equity multiples, challenges small-cap refinancing math, lifts the dollar, and pushes real yields high enough that even gold can fall during a geopolitical scare.

## How It Spread

US equities behaved exactly like a duration shock. The S&P 500 lost 1.24%, the Nasdaq fell 1.54%, and the Russell 2000 dropped 2.44%. Energy was the exception because the original shock was crude-positive. Everything else had to decide whether earnings power could outrun a higher discount rate. High-multiple growth and rate-sensitive small caps could not.

Europe confirmed the global transmission. The STOXX 600 fell 1.48%, the DAX lost 2.07%, Bund yields were near 15-year-high territory around 3.15%, and UK gilts reached toward 5.185% amid local political risk. The analyst's ECB-hike framing is important: Europe is not simply importing US rates; it is importing the energy-inflation problem into its own policy path.

Asia added a third channel. The KOSPI reversed violently after breaching 8,000 and closed near 7,493, while the Nikkei fell almost 2% under Bank of Japan tightening pressure. China was steadier after the Trump-Xi summit, but the summit delivered stabilization rather than a new growth catalyst. That left Asia exposed to the same global yield and positioning unwind as everyone else.

## Why Sentiment Has Not Broken

The useful nuance is that investors have not capitulated. CNN Fear & Greed remained at 63, AAII bullishness was only moderately above average, and ETF net issuance stayed positive. But VIX rose to 18.43 and put/call behavior showed protection buying. This is hedged greed: investors want to stay in the bull market, but they no longer trust the inflation and rates backdrop enough to run unprotected.

That makes the next move highly conditional. If oil gaps lower on de-escalation and the 10-year falls back below the 4.55%-4.60% zone, Friday can be remembered as a healthy insurance reset. If Brent pushes through $110-$112 and the 10-year moves toward 4.65%, the same hedged positioning becomes fragile. The market can survive expensive oil; what it cannot easily absorb is expensive oil that keeps central banks trapped while growth-sensitive commodities are already dissenting.

**Conviction level:** Medium-high that the next 48 hours are governed by the oil-to-yields channel, not index momentum. The clean bullish trigger is lower crude plus lower yields; absent that, the stance remains Neutral-to-Risk-Off.
