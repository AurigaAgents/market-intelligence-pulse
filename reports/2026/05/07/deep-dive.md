# Deep Story — Risk-On, But Gold Did Not Believe the Whole Deal

The day's strongest story is the market's attempt to price a diplomatic off-ramp before it exists. A reported U.S.-Iran framework turned the Strait of Hormuz from a supply-crisis tail risk into a tradable peace premium. Brent's May 6 settle at $101.27, down 7.7%, and WTI's $96.21 settle, down 6.3%, were the anchor moves. Everything else followed from that: US records, European relief, Asia's catch-up rally, lower yields, and a softer dollar.

The equity reaction was broad enough to matter. In the US, the S&P 500 closed at 7,365.12, the Nasdaq at 25,838.94, and the Russell 2000 at 2,886.77, all record prints in the approved report. That is not just a megacap story. AMD's data-center guidance and the AI-capex complex explain the Nasdaq's outperformance, but Industrials, Materials, and small caps needed the oil/yield shock to fade. The market bought both themes at once: AI as earnings growth, Iran de-escalation as macro relief.

Europe showed the same logic in reverse composition. The STOXX 600 jumped 2.22% on May 6, then slipped modestly May 7 as investors took profit. That is consolidation, not rejection. Brent hovering around the $100 line hurt the energy-heavy FTSE but helped the continental inflation story. Bund yields below 3% are the quiet confirmation: if the market believes the oil shock is fading, it can price less inflation pressure and more support for duration-sensitive sectors.

Asia made the repricing look spectacular. Japan's Nikkei 225 surged 5.6% to 62,833.84 after Golden Week, absorbing three closed sessions of US records, Iran headlines, and AI-chip momentum in one day. Korea's KOSPI also reached a record, led by mega-cap AI and memory exposure. But China lagged, with CSI 300 up only 0.48%. That split matters. The Asian message was not a synchronized regional boom; it was a global-risk and AI catch-up trade concentrated in the markets most sensitive to those factors.

Then comes the contradiction: gold and silver stayed bid. Spot gold near $4,702 and silver near $77.83 do not fit a clean all-clear narrative. They say investors are willing to buy equities if the oil tail risk fades, but they are not willing to sell the insurance tied to de-dollarization, tariffs, real assets, and policy uncertainty. The dollar's slip to 98.03 reinforces the same point. The safe-haven currency premium eased, yet hard-asset hedges remained useful.

Sentiment makes the rally more durable, but not invulnerable. CNN Fear & Greed at 68.3 and a low equity put/call ratio show greed and limited hedging demand. Yet AAII's bull-bear spread is still negative, and QQQ inflows look more institutional than retail. This is not a full household capitulation into stocks. Rallies can keep climbing that wall of skepticism.

The next confirmation belongs to crude. If Brent breaks below $100 and holds, equities have fundamental backing: lower input costs, lower inflation pass-through, and less emergency risk premium. If Brent rebounds toward $108-$110, the peace premium was premature. Then gold was not the contradiction. It was the warning.
