# Markets Overview — 2026-05-07

## So What

Markets have moved from pricing the Iran/Hormuz shock as a one-way supply crisis to treating diplomacy as the new base case. The repricing is broad: Brent's May 6 collapse pulled yields lower, sent US stocks to fresh records, let Europe digest a 2% rally with only mild profit-taking, and gave Japan a violent Golden Week catch-up bid. But the tape is not clean euphoria. Gold and silver are still firm, retail sentiment remains skeptical, and the whole structure depends on a U.S.-Iran framework that is not yet signed. The day's message: risk-on, but with a geopolitical circuit breaker.

## Risk Dashboard

1. **The deal is still a headline, not a settlement.** Markets are treating the reported one-page U.S.-Iran framework as high probability, while Trump simultaneously described signing as too soon and kept military pressure in the background. If Tehran rejects the framework or the 48-hour response window slips, the first reversal should appear in Brent and WTI.

2. **Brent $100 is the truth test.** A sustained break below $100 would validate the disinflation and supply-relief narrative. A rebound through $108-$110 would tell equities that the peace premium was marked too aggressively.

3. **Record highs reduce the margin for error.** The S&P, Nasdaq, Russell 2000, Nikkei, and KOSPI all printed or approached records. That confirms breadth, but it also means the market is entering the next Iran headline and May 12 CPI setup from stretched levels.

4. **Gold refuses to confirm a full risk-on regime.** Spot gold near $4,700 and silver near $78 alongside record equities is not classic complacency. It signals that real-asset and de-dollarization hedges remain alive beneath the equity melt-up.

5. **Asia's biggest move may be catch-up, not new alpha.** The Nikkei's historic point gain partly reflects three closed sessions being priced at once. Follow-through matters more than the first reopen print.

6. **Sentiment can still extend, but the trigger has changed.** Fear & Greed in the high 60s is warm, not extreme. The late-cycle warning would be AAII bulls surging above 50% while put/call drops toward 0.50 and VIX stays compressed.

## Cross-Segment Synthesis

The common driver is supply-risk compression. The market did not simply decide that growth improved overnight; it decided that the most dangerous energy-shipping tail risk was less likely. That repricing lowered crude, softened the dollar, eased yields, and let investors rotate into the assets that had been most penalized by Hormuz fear: European cyclicals, Asian importers, US small caps, and AI-linked growth.

The second driver is AI-capex momentum. AMD's data-center revenue growth and guidance gave the US rally a hard earnings anchor, then spilled into Japan's Softbank/Ibiden complex and Korea's Samsung-centered leadership. This is why the Nasdaq outperformed and why Korea continues to look exceptional. But the AI factor did not explain the whole tape. Industrials, Materials, Russell 2000, Bunds, oil, and the FTSE's energy drag all point back to Iran.

The third driver is unresolved hedging. If this were pure euphoria, gold would be weaker, retail would be chasing, and volatility would be closer to the low-teens floor. Instead, VIX is compressed but not dead, AAII is still negative, and metals are strong. That makes the rally healthier in one sense and more conditional in another: there is room for performance chasing if headlines stay constructive, but there is no evidence that investors have forgotten the risk.

Net regime: **risk-on with a crude-price circuit breaker and a metals hedge.** Below $100 Brent, the rally can broaden again. Above $110 Brent, the entire May 6-7 move starts to look like a premature peace-deal trade.
