# Global Markets - May 27, 2026

## Opening Take

The so what today: markets are buying two forms of relief at once, and both are still conditional. The first is the peace dividend: WTI near $89.62 and Brent near $95.80 say the Hormuz risk premium is being drained as investors price a possible U.S.-Iran settlement. The second is the AI hardware bid: Micron's move into the trillion-dollar club, Korea's record KOSPI close, and semiconductor strength across Japan, Europe, and the U.S. kept risk appetite alive even while China and retail sentiment lagged. The global stance is risk-on, but narrow and headline-sensitive.

## What Drove the Day

**1. AI hardware became the cleanest equity engine.** The U.S. report's strongest signal was not simply Nasdaq strength. It was the breadth inside the semiconductor complex: Micron, AMD, ON, KLA, Lam Research, and Applied Materials all showed that the AI capex narrative is still attracting institutional money. Asia confirmed the same regime. Korea's KOSPI closed at a record 8,228.70 after crossing 8,400 intraday, helped by SK Hynix value creation and Samsung's provisional wage deal. Europe caught a smaller echo through X-Fab and related chip momentum.

**2. Oil relief broadened the macro bid.** Iran's pledge to restore Hormuz shipping after a deal pulled energy lower and changed the inflation math. WTI below $90 is the practical signal for equities; Brent near $96 is the reminder that the premium has not fully vanished. Europe was the clearest beneficiary because lower energy costs directly support industrial margins, airlines, and consumer discretionary names. The FTSE's underperformance showed the other side: cheaper crude hurts energy-heavy indices.

**3. Bonds helped, but did not take over.** U.S. 10-year yields near 4.49% eased pressure on equity multiples and supported the small-cap breakout. Bunds around 2.975% and a modest Italy-Germany spread kept Europe calm. The June central-bank cluster now matters more because a confirmed oil drop would weaken the inflation hawks' case, while a failed Iran deal would restore the exact pressure markets just started to fade.

**4. Sentiment is comfortable on the surface and uneasy underneath.** CNN's Fear & Greed at 66, VIX below 17, and a low equity put/call ratio say investors are not paying up for broad downside protection. AAII says something different: bulls fell to 31.7%, bears rose to 43.6%, and the bull-bear spread dropped to -11.9 points. That split is not bearish by itself, but it makes the rally dependent on volatility staying pinned.

## Cross-Segment Synthesis

Today's market is **risk-on with two narrow engines**. The peace engine lowers energy costs and gives central banks a better path. The AI hardware engine provides direct earnings momentum and clear leadership. Together they explain why U.S. small caps, European cyclicals, Korean chips, and volatility-sensitive assets could all improve at once.

The weakness is concentration. The Asia file is explicit: this was a North Asia chip rally, not a broad Asian bid. The U.S. file says even within technology, software was mixed while semiconductors captured flows. The sentiment file shows headline greed but retail caution. The macro file shows copper near highs, oil down, and gold lower -- a constructive mix, but one that assumes de-escalation keeps working.

So the next session's test is straightforward. If PCE is benign, Brent stays below $100, and chip leadership survives earnings scrutiny, the rally can broaden. If any one of those pillars fails, May 27 will look less like a durable breakout and more like a narrow relief trade wrapped around a crowded AI story.
