# Markets Are Buying The Diplomacy Dividend Before The Deal Exists

The day's strongest story is that investors are treating diplomacy itself as a macro asset. The approved bundle does not show a completed US-Iran deal, a permanent Hormuz reopening, or a settled U.S.-EU trade framework. It shows markets moving because the probability distribution shifted. That was enough to knock Brent down to $98.49, pull WTI to $91.63, support European cyclicals, and leave Asian risk appetite intact while the US was closed for Memorial Day.

Oil is the transmission channel. For weeks the Middle East risk premium linked every asset class: higher crude meant higher inflation risk, higher long yields, more pressure on consumers, and less room for central banks. On May 25, that chain loosened. The macro report attributes the roughly 5% crude drop to progress toward a US-Iran framework, including a possible path toward supply normalization around the Strait of Hormuz. The key phrase is possible path. The deal is not complete, but futures markets do not wait for signatures when the marginal risk has changed.

That oil move also explains why equities could look through other discomforts. In the US, the last live cash session already had a constructive setup: S&P 500 at 7,473.47, Dow at a record 50,579.70, Russell 2000 up 0.91%, and the 10-year yield easing to 4.56%. A lower oil impulse makes that Friday breadth easier to defend when US trading resumes. It reduces the chance that the 30-year yield above 5% becomes an immediate equity shock.

Europe was the live test case. The DAX rose to 25,261.20 and the CAC to 8,248 as tariff-risk relief and a steady ECB backdrop let investors add cyclical beta. Importantly, EUR/USD at 1.1641 means this was not a weak-currency export rally. Europe rose even with a firm euro, suggesting the driver was policy-risk compression rather than simple FX math. That is a better-quality signal, but it is also more fragile: if tariff headlines reverse, the support can disappear quickly.

Asia gave the same story a momentum overlay. The Nikkei closed above 65,000, KOSPI held a modest positive correction after stale data were fixed, and Shanghai gained while the yuan strengthened. Hong Kong still lagged, so this was not indiscriminate China euphoria. It was a selective risk-on response to softer dollar pressure, calmer oil, and a market willing to believe policy risk is becoming less hostile.

Sentiment is the useful warning. VIX at 16.70 and CNN Fear & Greed around 59 say the market-price gauges are calm. AAII says households are not: bullishness fell to 31.7%, bearishness rose to 43.6%, and the bull-bear spread dropped to -11.9. That split is why the diplomacy dividend can keep working. Skepticism leaves room for a wall-of-worry rally if the headlines keep improving. But it also means conviction is shallow.

The next test is brutal and simple. If Brent stays below $100 and Tuesday's US reopen confirms Europe and Asia, the market can treat diplomacy as a real disinflation impulse. If talks stall and crude snaps back toward Friday's $103.54 Brent reference, the same trades become exposed: European cyclicals, Japan momentum, small caps, and AI-duration names all lean on lower macro stress. Markets bought the peace dividend early. Now the diplomats have to earn the price.
