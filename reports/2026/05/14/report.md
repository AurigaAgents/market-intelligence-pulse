## Overview

**So what:** The global tape is still risk-on, but the quality of that risk appetite deteriorated. US and European indices pushed higher on AI and mega-cap technology leadership even as inflation, yields, and central-bank risk stayed uncomfortable. Asia split three ways: Korea broke to fresh records, Japan reversed from an intraday high, and China/Hong Kong faded after the Trump-Xi optics failed to become a concrete catalyst. Macro conditions improved only at the margin because Hormuz is no longer priced as fully closed; it is now a selective, rationed chokepoint. That is less acute than yesterday's panic, but it is not normal.

## Cross-Asset Dashboard

**Equities:** The equity story is narrow leadership, not broad reflation. The US S&P 500 at 7,444.25 and Nasdaq at 26,402.34 verified as fresh highs in the US bundle, but the reviewer kept the Dow caveat alive: some feeds had the Dow lower while the analyst table used Yahoo/CNBC's flatter print. That caveat does not change the conclusion. Mega-cap AI remains the engine; equal-weight and defensive breadth are not confirming with the same force.

Europe joined the AI bid, led by the DAX around 24,464 and STOXX 600 near 611. The important nuance is that Europe rallied into tighter policy risk. Bund 10Y near 3.08% and ECB hike discussion mean the equity move is cyclically optimistic rather than rate-supported. A DAX close above 24,700 would invite momentum buying, but the same trade becomes vulnerable if Bund yields break 3.10%.

Asia refused to confirm a single regime. The final, reviewed cycle-2 Asia report fixed the stale cycle-1 KOSPI issue: Korea's KOSPI closed at 7,981.41, +1.75%, just shy of 8,000, with Samsung up more than 4%. Japan's Nikkei fell 0.98% to 62,654.05 after touching 63,799 intraday, while Shanghai fell 1.52%. That is not Asia-wide risk-on; it is AI-led Korea, intervention-sensitive Japan, and summit-fatigued China.

**Rates, commodities, and FX:** The biggest correction versus the first macro draft is Hormuz. The accepted cycle-2 macro report no longer treats the strait as effectively closed. Reuters-syndicated and related sources showed roughly 30 vessel transits plus a Chinese-tanker corridor. Brent therefore traded around $105.64, down from the prior close, not up in a fresh supply panic. The macro risk remains real because the chokepoint is politically rationed, but the price signal shifted from hard disruption to controlled bypass.

Inflation did not improve. April CPI at 3.8% year over year, April PPI at +1.4% month over month, US 20Y around 5.00%, and a firm dollar keep pressure on duration assets. Silver's drop near 3.2% while gold stayed above $4,700 is the cleanest tell: the market is distinguishing yield-sensitive metals from defensive stores of value. Bitcoin around $79.6K–$80.1K is behaving like a risk asset, not an inflation hedge.

## Sentiment and Positioning

The headline sentiment read says greed; the internals say fragility. CNN Fear & Greed near 65.8 is supported by extreme-greed momentum and put-call components, but breadth and junk-bond demand remain weak. AAII is more important than the headline: bulls and bears both rose while neutrals collapsed to 24.1%. Investors are not calmly agreeing on a soft landing; they are polarizing.

That leaves the tape vulnerable to a volatility air pocket. VIX at 17.55 and CBOE equity put/call near 0.50 imply hedge-light positioning. If inflation, Fed rhetoric, or Hormuz headlines turn negative, the downside would likely be abrupt rather than gradual.

## Forward Outlook

The base case for the next session is continued selective risk-taking: long AI/semis, cautious on China large caps, and alert to rate-sensitive reversals. The two decisive cross-market triggers are: first, whether Hormuz partial transit expands into a broader reopening or becomes a durable political carve-out; second, whether US yields push through the 4.50% 10Y / 5.00% 20Y zone with equities still at highs.

The contrarian read is that the market is treating each risk as isolated — hot inflation, narrow breadth, ECB hawkishness, China disappointment, and a rationed oil chokepoint. The stronger interpretation is that they are connected by tightening liquidity. Risk can still rise when liquidity tightens, but it becomes a concentration trade. Today's market is up, not healthy.

## Deep Dive

The most important market story today is not that equities went up. It is that they went up while nearly every quality signal got worse. The S&P 500 and Nasdaq printed fresh highs, Europe followed the Nvidia-led technology impulse, and Korea pushed the KOSPI to a record just below 8,000. On the surface, this is a global risk-on day. Underneath, it is a narrow liquidity trade with inflation, rates, credit, breadth, and oil logistics all arguing for less confidence than the index tape suggests.

Start with the US. The approved US report verified the core contradiction: CPI at 3.8% year over year, hot PPI, and the 10-year Treasury near 4.47% should normally pressure long-duration growth stocks. Instead, Nasdaq leadership absorbed the hit. That is bullish in the short term because price action matters; it is also unstable because the rally is concentrating in the exact assets most exposed if real yields keep rising. The review allowed the Dow feed discrepancy because it was not material. The material point is narrower: cap-weighted technology is strong, breadth is less convincing.

Europe shows the same pattern in a different currency. The DAX rallied on AI beta and a weaker euro, but Bund yields near 3.08% and ECB hike risk mean European equities are not being lifted by easier money. They are rallying despite tighter expected policy. That can work for a while if earnings momentum stays strong, but it compresses the margin of safety. A move through 3.10% in Bund yields would be a cleaner warning than a small one-day equity pullback.

Asia makes the concentration problem visible. Korea was the clear winner because the AI hardware supply chain still has a bid; Samsung gained more than 4% and the KOSPI closed at 7,981.41. Japan reversed from a new intraday high as yen weakness and BoJ normalization risk mattered again. China and Hong Kong faded after the Trump-Xi summit failed to deliver enough concrete policy relief. There is no uniform Asia risk-on regime here. There is Korea AI, Japan rate anxiety, and China disappointment.

Macro did improve versus the first draft, but only because the market walked back the worst version of the Hormuz story. The accepted cycle-2 macro report corrected the key error: the strait is not effectively closed. Roughly 30 vessels transited and a Chinese-tanker corridor appears to exist. That pulled Brent toward $105.64 instead of validating a fresh supply-panic thesis. But this is not a benign reopening. A politically rationed chokepoint can still keep a structural war premium in oil even when prices fade from the peak.

Sentiment ties it together. CNN Fear & Greed around 66 says Greed, but the internals split between extreme-greed momentum/put-call signals and weak breadth/credit signals. AAII is polarized: bulls and bears both rose while neutrals collapsed. VIX at 17.55 and equity put/call near 0.50 say investors are lightly hedged. That combination is not euphoria; it is fragility.

The tradeable conclusion is simple. Risk can keep working, but it should be treated as a concentration trade, not a clean macro all-clear. The strongest expression remains selective AI exposure. The hedge is not necessarily short equities; it is protection against the liquidity variables that the equity tape is ignoring: yields, dollar strength, credit breadth, and a Hormuz regime that is controlled rather than resolved.
