# Deep Story — Risk-On With a Liquidity Warning Label

The most important market story today is not that equities went up. It is that they went up while nearly every quality signal got worse. The S&P 500 and Nasdaq printed fresh highs, Europe followed the Nvidia-led technology impulse, and Korea pushed the KOSPI to a record just below 8,000. On the surface, this is a global risk-on day. Underneath, it is a narrow liquidity trade with inflation, rates, credit, breadth, and oil logistics all arguing for less confidence than the index tape suggests.

Start with the US. The approved US report verified the core contradiction: CPI at 3.8% year over year, hot PPI, and the 10-year Treasury near 4.47% should normally pressure long-duration growth stocks. Instead, Nasdaq leadership absorbed the hit. That is bullish in the short term because price action matters; it is also unstable because the rally is concentrating in the exact assets most exposed if real yields keep rising. The review allowed the Dow feed discrepancy because it was not material. The material point is narrower: cap-weighted technology is strong, breadth is less convincing.

Europe shows the same pattern in a different currency. The DAX rallied on AI beta and a weaker euro, but Bund yields near 3.08% and ECB hike risk mean European equities are not being lifted by easier money. They are rallying despite tighter expected policy. That can work for a while if earnings momentum stays strong, but it compresses the margin of safety. A move through 3.10% in Bund yields would be a cleaner warning than a small one-day equity pullback.

Asia makes the concentration problem visible. Korea was the clear winner because the AI hardware supply chain still has a bid; Samsung gained more than 4% and the KOSPI closed at 7,981.41. Japan reversed from a new intraday high as yen weakness and BoJ normalization risk mattered again. China and Hong Kong faded after the Trump-Xi summit failed to deliver enough concrete policy relief. There is no uniform Asia risk-on regime here. There is Korea AI, Japan rate anxiety, and China disappointment.

Macro did improve versus the first draft, but only because the market walked back the worst version of the Hormuz story. The accepted cycle-2 macro report corrected the key error: the strait is not effectively closed. Roughly 30 vessels transited and a Chinese-tanker corridor appears to exist. That pulled Brent toward $105.64 instead of validating a fresh supply-panic thesis. But this is not a benign reopening. A politically rationed chokepoint can still keep a structural war premium in oil even when prices fade from the peak.

Sentiment ties it together. CNN Fear & Greed around 66 says Greed, but the internals split between extreme-greed momentum/put-call signals and weak breadth/credit signals. AAII is polarized: bulls and bears both rose while neutrals collapsed. VIX at 17.55 and equity put/call near 0.50 say investors are lightly hedged. That combination is not euphoria; it is fragility.

The tradeable conclusion is simple. Risk can keep working, but it should be treated as a concentration trade, not a clean macro all-clear. The strongest expression remains selective AI exposure. The hedge is not necessarily short equities; it is protection against the liquidity variables that the equity tape is ignoring: yields, dollar strength, credit breadth, and a Hormuz regime that is controlled rather than resolved.
