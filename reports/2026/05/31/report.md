## Overview

## So what

Global markets enter June in a risk-on regime, but the signal is conditional rather than broad. Friday's completed cash session left the S&P 500 and Nasdaq at records, the STOXX 600 near its highs, Japan and Korea sharply higher, and VIX down near 15. The common catalyst was a possible US-Iran ceasefire extension that pulled energy risk premium out of crude and let investors keep adding equity exposure. The catch is that the rally is doing two different things at once: rewarding AI infrastructure aggressively while refusing to buy every cyclical asset. Nine of eleven US sectors fell on Friday, Shanghai declined, European gains were thin, retail investors stayed net bearish, and sticky inflation kept rates from confirming a clean easing story.

## Cross-segment synthesis

The day's cleanest through-line is the removal of oil tail risk. WTI fell roughly 1.7% into the high-$87s, Brent traded near $91-$92, and macro desks framed the move as the latest leg of a multi-week geopolitical-risk unwind. That helped equities, airlines, oil importers, and volatility sellers. It also softened the dollar: DXY stayed below 99, EUR/USD reached 1.1644, and gold still held near $4,550 as the weaker-dollar channel offset the usual peace-trade pressure on havens.

But the equity transmission was selective. In the US, Dell's record 32.8% surge after stronger AI-computing guidance was large enough to pull the S&P 500 green while most sectors closed red. Nasdaq strength and Microsoft participation say the AI capex cycle remains the market's preferred growth engine. Asia confirmed that from the hardware side: KOSPI gained 3.55% to a record 8,476.15, helped by Samsung/HBM news, while the Nikkei added 2.53%. That is not a generic global reflation trade; it is a specific rerating of servers, memory, hyperscaler demand, and adjacent infrastructure.

Europe shows why the label stays conditional. The STOXX 600 rose only 0.14%, with Italy and Spain doing more work than the DAX, CAC, or FTSE. Airlines liked lower Brent, defense still carried a separate Ukraine/Romania risk bid, and luxury participated, but the larger regional story is rates. Euro-zone inflation stayed above the ECB target for a third month, money markets shifted toward 50 bp of tightening by year-end, and the Bund held near 2.95%. Europe gets the peace dividend, but not a free discount-rate pass.

The same discipline applies in the US. April core PCE at 3.8% is too hot for a clean Fed-cut narrative, even if falling oil improves the forward inflation impulse. The 10-year near 4.45% says bonds are not fighting the equity rally, but they are not underwriting it either. That matters because sentiment is already constructive: CNN Fear & Greed reads 60, VIX is near the mid-15s, and equity put/call at 0.42 shows low single-stock hedging demand. Retail remains net bearish, so this is not full euphoria, but institutional complacency has plainly returned.

Base case: constructive into June if the truce holds, oil stays heavy, and AI leadership keeps broadening into hardware and infrastructure. The failure mode is equally clear: a single geopolitical reversal or hot macro print can expose how much of the rally rests on narrow leadership and compressed volatility.

## Deep Dive

## The hook

The dominant market story is the same across assets: investors are treating a possible US-Iran ceasefire extension as real enough to remove risk premium, but not broad enough to erase every macro constraint. Oil fell again, volatility compressed, US equities printed records, Europe held near highs, and Japan/Korea rallied hard. That is a peace dividend. The narrowness matters: the money did not flow evenly into everything. It flowed into AI infrastructure, oil importers, airlines, selected defense names, and volatility-selling exposures. China lagged, most US sectors fell, and rates stayed stubborn.

## What changed

Crude is the transmission channel. WTI closed in the high-$87s, down about 1.7% on the day, while Brent traded around $91-$92. The market read the US-Iran truce headlines as a lower-probability Strait-of-Hormuz shock, so energy inflation risk came out of the front end. That helped risk assets immediately: lower oil makes airline margins easier, reduces the worst-case inflation tail, and gives systematic strategies room to add exposure when VIX is near 15.

The second channel is the dollar. DXY sat just under 99 and EUR/USD reached 1.1644. A softer dollar helped gold hold around $4,550 despite the peace narrative, and it supported the global-risk frame for equities. In isolation, that looks like a classic risk-on setup: oil lower, dollar softer, volatility lower, indexes higher.

## Why it stayed selective

The selection effect is the real signal. In the US, Dell jumped 32.8% after raising guidance on AI-computing demand, and Microsoft gained 5.5%. The S&P 500 rose for a ninth straight week, but nine of eleven sectors were red on Friday. That is not a broad cyclical melt-up. It is an AI-capex market using lower geopolitical risk as fuel.

Asia told the same story. Korea's KOSPI surged 3.55% to a record 8,476.15, helped by Samsung/HBM optimism. Japan's Nikkei rose 2.53%. Hang Seng participated with a 0.70% gain, but Shanghai fell 0.73%. The market is willing to pay for hardware and AI supply-chain exposure; it is not automatically buying mainland China growth.

Europe adds the policy brake. The STOXX 600 rose only 0.14% even with the peace bid. Airlines liked lower oil, periphery markets outperformed, and defense still had its own Ukraine/Romania catalyst. But euro-zone inflation stayed above target for a third month, Bund yields held around 2.95%, and markets shifted toward ECB tightening by year-end. Europe can receive the oil dividend and still be capped by rates.

## Our take

The peace trade is investable, but not self-sustaining. It needs three things to keep working: no weekend geopolitical reversal, oil failing to reclaim the low-to-mid $90s, and AI infrastructure leadership continuing to validate earnings expectations. If those hold, the path of least resistance remains higher and VIX can stay in the 14-18 zone.

The risk is that investors confuse reduced tail risk with a solved macro problem. April core PCE at 3.8% and a US 10-year near 4.45% do not say “all clear.” CNN Fear & Greed at 60 and an equity put/call ratio of 0.42 say hedges have already been cut. Retail bearishness keeps this below euphoria, but institutional complacency is visible.

So the market's message is precise: buy lower geopolitical risk, but do not pretend it fixed breadth, inflation, or China. The peace dividend is real. Its margin of safety is narrow.

**Conviction:** Medium bullish while oil stays heavy and AI infrastructure leads; cautious on chasing index highs without breadth confirmation.
