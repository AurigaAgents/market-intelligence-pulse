# Global Markets — May 31, 2026

## So what

Global markets enter June in a risk-on regime, but the signal is conditional rather than broad. Friday's completed cash session left the S&P 500 and Nasdaq at records, the STOXX 600 near its highs, Japan and Korea sharply higher, and VIX down near 15. The common catalyst was a possible US-Iran ceasefire extension that pulled energy risk premium out of crude and let investors keep adding equity exposure. The catch is that the rally is doing two different things at once: rewarding AI infrastructure aggressively while refusing to buy every cyclical asset. Nine of eleven US sectors fell on Friday, Shanghai declined, European gains were thin, retail investors stayed net bearish, and sticky inflation kept rates from confirming a clean easing story.

## Cross-segment synthesis

The day's cleanest through-line is the removal of oil tail risk. WTI fell roughly 1.7% into the high-$87s, Brent traded near $91-$92, and macro desks framed the move as the latest leg of a multi-week geopolitical-risk unwind. That helped equities, airlines, oil importers, and volatility sellers. It also softened the dollar: DXY stayed below 99, EUR/USD reached 1.1644, and gold still held near $4,550 as the weaker-dollar channel offset the usual peace-trade pressure on havens.

But the equity transmission was selective. In the US, Dell's record 32.8% surge after stronger AI-computing guidance was large enough to pull the S&P 500 green while most sectors closed red. Nasdaq strength and Microsoft participation say the AI capex cycle remains the market's preferred growth engine. Asia confirmed that from the hardware side: KOSPI gained 3.55% to a record 8,476.15, helped by Samsung/HBM news, while the Nikkei added 2.53%. That is not a generic global reflation trade; it is a specific rerating of servers, memory, hyperscaler demand, and adjacent infrastructure.

Europe shows why the label stays conditional. The STOXX 600 rose only 0.14%, with Italy and Spain doing more work than the DAX, CAC, or FTSE. Airlines liked lower Brent, defense still carried a separate Ukraine/Romania risk bid, and luxury participated, but the larger regional story is rates. Euro-zone inflation stayed above the ECB target for a third month, money markets shifted toward 50 bp of tightening by year-end, and the Bund held near 2.95%. Europe gets the peace dividend, but not a free discount-rate pass.

The same discipline applies in the US. April core PCE at 3.8% is too hot for a clean Fed-cut narrative, even if falling oil improves the forward inflation impulse. The 10-year near 4.45% says bonds are not fighting the equity rally, but they are not underwriting it either. That matters because sentiment is already constructive: CNN Fear & Greed reads 60, VIX is near the mid-15s, and equity put/call at 0.42 shows low single-stock hedging demand. Retail remains net bearish, so this is not full euphoria, but institutional complacency has plainly returned.

Base case: constructive into June if the truce holds, oil stays heavy, and AI leadership keeps broadening into hardware and infrastructure. The failure mode is equally clear: a single geopolitical reversal or hot macro print can expose how much of the rally rests on narrow leadership and compressed volatility.
