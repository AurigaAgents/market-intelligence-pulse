# Deep Story — The Market Is Trading Policy Divergence, Not Just Hormuz

The easiest story today is renewed Iran/Hormuz risk: Brent is back around $100-$101, Europe is soft, Asia closed cautious, and the first layer of ceasefire optimism has faded. That story is true, but it is not sufficient. The day's more important move is that markets are absorbing the oil risk through a policy-divergence lens. Strong US labor did not push US yields higher, the dollar stayed near 52-week lows, the euro pressed toward 1.18, and gold plus copper rallied together. That is not a simple war scare. It is a market repricing the relative credibility of central banks and the scarcity value of real assets.

The US signal is the cleanest. The approved US report notes a May 8 payrolls beat, yet US10Y eased to about 4.36% and US2Y to roughly 3.88%. In a classic inflation-fear regime, hot labor would hit duration and pressure growth equities. Instead, the bond market treated the data as soft-landing confirmation: growth is alive, but not hot enough to cancel the rate-cut path. That is why the US equity weakness reads as distribution after highs, not a broken regime. Thursday's close was red, small caps lagged, and the S&P reversed from a fresh high, but VIX remained near 17.

Europe is the mirror image. EUR/USD near 1.178 is not just dollar weakness; it reflects a market pricing a more hawkish ECB, with more than 50 bp of tightening into year-end in the approved EU report. That is difficult when oil risk is rising. The DAX and CAC sold off more than the FTSE because continental exporters and industrials carry the imported-energy shock directly, while the FTSE's oil majors and miners hedge it. The same Brent price that hurts German margins can support London index composition.

Asia makes the distinction between shock and trend clearer. Nikkei slipped only 0.19% after a strong week, and KOSPI still closed fractionally higher after a 13.6% five-day surge. If Iran risk were the whole story, Korea would not be so resilient. AI/semi demand and a softer regional FX backdrop still matter. China and Hong Kong were weaker, but that weakness came alongside a firmer yuan, suggesting policy restraint and domestic-sector drag rather than pure global panic.

The macro stack seals it. Gold near $4,735 is easy to explain as a haven. Copper at $6.24/lb, up strongly, is not. When gold, silver, and copper all hold bid while DXY sits near 97.96 and Bitcoin fails near $79k, the market is not simply hiding. It is paying for hard assets tied to debasement, reflation, and supply constraint, while refusing to chase every speculative beta expression.

That is why Brent $100 is the circuit breaker rather than the whole regime. Below $100, investors can treat oil as a fading tax and lean into weak-dollar reflation. Above $103-$110, the oil shock would start to contaminate inflation expectations, European margins, and Asia import costs. Today's report sits between those worlds: equity indices are tired, but the underlying macro trade is still alive.

The caveat is sentiment. The final sentiment report was rejected because its SPX/RUT breadth math used bad baselines; the corrected reviewer read showed both indices positive intraday, with Russell merely underperforming. So the warning is not a small-cap crash. It is warmer positioning — Fear & Greed near 68, put/call at 0.46 — meeting a market whose next leg depends on whether policy divergence remains supportive or oil turns from circuit breaker into load-bearing shock.
