# Global Markets — May 30, 2026

## So what

Friday's close left global markets in a risk-on regime, but not a clean one. US equities set fresh records, Asia rallied hard, oil and gold fell on a tentative US-Iran truce extension, and sentiment moved into Greed. The catch is breadth: the bid is concentrated in AI infrastructure, semiconductors, and relief from lower energy risk. Small caps slipped, China fell while neighbors rallied, and the US 10-year yield stayed pinned near 4.45% after sticky PCE. This is not a broad soft-landing celebration; it is a crowded AI-capex trade wrapped in geopolitical relief.

## Cross-segment synthesis

The day's cleanest cross-market connection is lower tail risk. The tentative US-Iran 60-day truce extension, still pending President Trump's approval, hit oil first: WTI dropped to a five-week low and crude logged its largest monthly decline of 2026. That helped oil importers, pressured energy-heavy indexes, pushed gold lower, and gave equity investors permission to keep bidding risk.

But the equity leadership was not simply “peace trade.” It was AI capex. In the US, Dell's best day on record and Snowflake's prior-session surge confirmed that hyperscaler spending is being capitalized across servers, software, storage, and observability. Asia showed the same theme in hardware form: Samsung closed +5.84% after HBM4E sample-shipment headlines, the KOSPI hit a record 8,476.15, and Lenovo surged nearly 22% in Hong Kong. The story is no longer just Mag-7 mega-cap concentration; it is the full infrastructure stack being rerated.

That makes the weak spots more important. The Russell 2000 fell 0.59% even as the S&P 500 rose for a seventh straight day. US sector breadth was poor intraday, with most S&P sectors red despite the index close. Shanghai lost 0.73% while Japan, Korea, Hong Kong, and Australia rallied, showing that local Chinese growth and policy doubts still override regional risk appetite. Europe was constructive but quiet: STOXX 600 +0.14%, DAX almost flat, and FTSE down as oil weakness hurt its commodity weight.

Rates are the discipline. April PCE was not a disaster, but headline inflation at 3.8% and core at 3.3% are still too sticky for a clean Fed-easing narrative. The US 10-year near 4.45% says the bond market has not joined the equity celebration. As long as yields stay there, the rally needs earnings momentum and lower oil to keep doing the work.

## Forward outlook

Next week is a three-part test. First, the Iran MoU either becomes a signed de-escalation or the oil move reverses. Second, US ISM and payrolls decide whether sticky PCE was a warning or background noise. Third, the ECB decision and Lagarde press conference test Europe's unusual mix of firm euro, lower Bund yields, and still-hawkish policy pricing. The base case is constructive but fragile: risk can extend if oil falls again and AI leadership holds, but the rally is vulnerable to any catalyst that exposes narrow breadth.
