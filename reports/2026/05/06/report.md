## So What

Markets priced the Iran/Hormuz shock as if the tail risk had moved from “shipping crisis” to “deal risk.” That single repricing drove the day: oil collapsed, Europe and Asia rallied, US futures and prior record closes stayed supported, and volatility remained calm rather than euphoric. The important caveat is timing. The market has bought the reported one-page U.S.–Iran framework before it is actually signed. That makes the tape risk-on, but conditional: the first asset to confirm or reject the story is still crude, not equities.

## Risk Dashboard

1. **The MoU is priced before it is delivered.** The equity rally and oil sell-off both rest on the same assumption: Washington and Tehran are close enough to a framework that Hormuz disruption risk can be marked down. If the framework slips, crude snaps first and equities follow.

2. **Brent near $100 is the market’s truth serum.** EU and Asia reports independently put Brent around $102 after a 6-7% fall, with intraday lows below $100 in some prints. A clean break below $100 would validate de-escalation; a rebound toward $108-$110 would say the market overpaid for headlines.

3. **Europe’s rally is composition-sensitive.** CAC outperformed on luxury and banks, while FTSE lagged because lower oil hurts heavyweight energy names. That is a good relief tape, not a uniform European growth upgrade. The DAX also failed to hold the 25,000 round-number zone.

4. **Asia’s next test is Tokyo.** Japan was closed while Korea, Hong Kong, and Shanghai repriced the deal story. Nikkei reopens into a stronger yen and two sessions of missed global relief; the gap direction will show whether Asia’s rally is broad or mostly Korea/Samsung-specific.

5. **Sentiment is supportive, not protective.** Fear & Greed in the high-60s and VIX in the high-teens allow momentum to continue. They do not provide much downside cushion if oil reverses. The warning signal would be Fear & Greed above 75, put/call near 0.70, and AAII bulls back above 45% together.

6. **Macro data must be treated with extra caution today.** The macro analyst cycle failed review on multiple core levels. The usable macro signal is the reviewer’s corrected one: lower oil, lower yields, softer dollar, gold above $4,700, and BTC near $81k. Do not carry forward the rejected “stagflation tightening” frame.

## Cross-Segment Synthesis

The day’s common thread is not “stocks up.” It is **supply-risk compression**. Crude was the source asset: as markets marked down the probability of a sustained Hormuz disruption, every importer-sensitive channel improved. Europe rallied because lower energy prices help margins and inflation expectations. Asia rallied because Korea, Hong Kong, and mainland China benefit directly from lower crude and a softer dollar. US equities were already at records and added pre-open support from the same oil relief, with semiconductors providing an idiosyncratic second engine.

The second thread is dispersion. CAC beat DAX and FTSE; KOSPI beat the rest of Asia by an order of magnitude; VOO took inflows while QQQ reportedly bled. That dispersion matters because it argues against a blind beta melt-up. Investors are reallocating toward the cleaner beneficiaries of the same macro story rather than buying everything indiscriminately.

The third thread is conditionality. Trump’s own caveat, cited in the Asia report, is that Iranian acceptance remains “perhaps a big assumption.” That sentence is the whole risk map. If the framework turns into a signed deal, the rally can broaden: oil-importers, travel, industrials, and banks get more room, while duration-sensitive growth benefits from lower yields. If it fails, the same trades unwind in reverse order: Brent first, then FTSE energy rebounds, then DAX/CAC autos and luxury fade, then US record-high positioning is exposed.

Net conditions are **risk-on with a crude-price circuit breaker**. The market’s message is constructive, but it is not settled. Brent below $100 would make the bullish case cleaner. Brent back above $108 would make today look like a headline squeeze rather than a regime shift.

## Deep Dive

The day’s most important market story is not that equities rallied. It is that markets treated the Strait of Hormuz shock as negotiable again. A reported one-page U.S.–Iran framework, plus the pause of the Hormuz escort program, was enough to make crude collapse and risk assets reprice across regions. That is a powerful signal, but also a fragile one: the market has moved from pricing disruption to pricing diplomacy before the agreement is actually signed.

Oil was the anchor. EU close-area checks put Brent near $102.4, down roughly 6.8%, after trading below $100 intraday in some prints. Asia’s bundle showed Brent around $102.5 and WTI around $96; the macro review later corrected WTI closer to $95, down about 7%. The exact live quote drift matters less than the direction and scale. A one-day oil move of that size is not normal noise. It is the market removing a war-and-shipping premium.

That removal propagated cleanly. Europe rallied hard because lower oil reduces the inflation and margin pressure that had been hanging over the tape. The CAC 40 led with a 2.94% gain, ahead of DAX at 2.24% and FTSE at 2.15%. That composition tells the story: Paris luxury and banks could rally directly on relief, while the FTSE’s energy weights partially diluted the benefit. The DAX also failed to hold 25,000, so Europe’s move was strong, but not indiscriminate.

Asia sent the same message with an importer accent. KOSPI surged 6.81% to 7,409.09, helped by Samsung and AI-memory enthusiasm, while Hang Seng rose 1.22% and Shanghai gained 1.17% on its post-holiday reopen. Korea was the exceptional tape, but Hong Kong’s positive close matters because it corrected the earlier false “Asia divergence” frame. The region was not risk-off; it was buying cheaper crude, a softer dollar, and a less dangerous Gulf headline.

The US side is more timing-sensitive because the approved report uses May 5 closes and May 6 pre-open context rather than a same-day cash close. Still, the direction fits the global pattern. The S&P 500 and Nasdaq had just printed record closes, VIX was verified at 17.38, and futures were bid as oil fell. Semiconductors added an idiosyncratic boost after reports linking Intel to possible Apple silicon for US-built devices. In other words, the US did not need the Hormuz story to create a rally, but it used the story to defend the record zone.

The macro review is the quiet guardrail. The rejected macro draft said oil up, dollar up, yields up, and Bitcoin down. Independent checks found the opposite: oil down, yields lower, DXY softer, gold above $4,700, and BTC near $81.5k. That configuration is not stagflation panic. It is relief with residual hedging. Gold staying firm while crude falls says investors are fading the immediate shipping shock, not abandoning all geopolitical and monetary hedges.

That is why the next move belongs to oil. If Brent breaks below $100 and stays there, today’s equity rally has fundamental confirmation: lower input costs, less inflation pressure, and less need for emergency hedging. If Brent rebounds toward $108-$110, the market has to admit it priced the MoU too early. Then the unwind is obvious: energy rebounds, Europe’s import-sensitive rally fades, Asia’s importer FX tailwind weakens, and US record-high positioning becomes exposed.

The market’s message is precise: diplomacy is now the base case, but not yet a fact. Today was the price of hope. Tomorrow needs evidence.
